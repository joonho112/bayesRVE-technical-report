6 Sandwich Variance Estimation

This chapter develops the complete theory of cluster-robust sandwich variance estimation as implemented in sandwich.R, the mathematical heart of bayesRVE. The sandwich estimator provides the frequentist-valid variance target that the Cholesky Calibration Correction (CCC, sec-ccc) uses to recalibrate Bayesian posterior draws. We cover the HC0 estimator, the CR2 bias correction of Bell & McCaffrey (2002), Satterthwaite degrees of freedom following Tipton (2015), and the CHE overlay pattern that unifies the correlated-effects (CE) and correlated-hierarchical-effects (CHE) models under a single computational framework.

The presentation is self-contained: a reader familiar with weighted least squares and basic matrix algebra can follow this chapter without reference to other parts of the report.

6.1 Why the Full-Joint Sandwich Is the Wrong Target

6.1.1 The Full Parameter Vector

In the CE conditional model (sec-ce-model), the full parameter vector is

\[ \boldsymbol{\psi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}', \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_J)' \in \mathbb{R}^{K+J}, \tag{6.1}\]

where \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) collects \(K\) fixed-effect coefficients and \(\theta_j\) are study-specific random intercepts. A naive approach would compute the full \((K+J) \times (K+J)\) sandwich:

\[ \mathbf{V}_{\text{sand}}^{\text{full}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}^{-1} \, \mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} \, \mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}^{-1}, \tag{6.2}\]

where \(\mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}\) is the posterior Hessian and \(\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j'\) is the outer-product meat. This approach fails for three distinct reasons.

The \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-block collapses. In the conditional model, \(\theta_j\) acts as a study-specific intercept that absorbs nearly all between-study variation. The \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-scores \(\partial \ell_j / \partial \boldsymbol{\beta}\) become nearly constant across studies, so the meat’s \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-block—which measures between-study heterogeneity of scores—is near zero. The sandwich would dramatically understate the true sampling variance of \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\).

Structural rank deficiency. The meat \(\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j'\) is a sum of \(J\) rank-one matrices, so \(\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}}) \leq J\). Since the parameter dimension is \(d = K + J > J\) (for \(K \geq 1\)), \(\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}}\) is structurally singular. A Cholesky factorization of the full sandwich, as CCC requires, is therefore impossible. This reflects the classical incidental-parameters problem (Neyman & Scott, 1948): with \(J\) nuisance parameters estimated from \(J\) clusters, the meat cannot span the full parameter space.

Cubic computational cost. Inverting a \((K+J) \times (K+J)\) Hessian costs \(O((K+J)^3)\), which becomes prohibitive as \(J\) grows into the hundreds.

6.1.2 The Blockwise Strategy

The correct approach, codified in Decision D2, is a blockwise hybrid target. The key insight is that \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) and \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) play fundamentally different roles: \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\) is the inferential target; \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) is a vector of nuisance parameters.

For the \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-block, we integrate out \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) to obtain a marginal model in which the between-study heterogeneity captured by \(\theta_j\) is absorbed into the marginal variance \(v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2\). The marginal sandwich then measures the correct quantity: the between-study variability of \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-scores under the marginal likelihood.

For the \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\)-block, we condition on \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) and exploit the fact that, given \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\), the \(\theta_j\) are conditionally independent. The conditional sandwich reduces to \(J\) scalar computations.

The CCC target is therefore block-diagonal:

\[ \mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\beta)} & \mathbf{0} \\ \mathbf{0} & \mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\theta)} \end{pmatrix}, \tag{6.3}\]

where \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\beta)}\) is a \(K \times K\) matrix from the marginal sandwich and \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\theta)}\) is a diagonal \(J \times J\) matrix from the conditional sandwich. The cross-block is set to zero by design (not estimation), reflecting the blockwise application of CCC (sec-ccc). This reduces the Cholesky cost from \(O((K+J)^3)\) to \(O(K^3 + J)\).

In sandwich.R, the marginal sandwich is computed by .marginal_sandwich() and the conditional sandwich by .conditional_sandwich(). The CHE model, where \(\boldsymbol{\theta}\) is analytically integrated out, requires only the marginal component (via .che_sandwich()). The final object is assembled by .assemble_bm_sandwich().

6.2 The Marginal Score and HC0 Sandwich

6.2.1 The CE Marginal Model

Integrating out \(\theta_j\) from the CE conditional model (sec-ce-model) yields the marginal distribution of study means:

\[ \bar{y}_j \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}, \tau^2 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j' \boldsymbol{\beta},\; v_j\right), \quad v_j = \frac{\bar{s}_j^2}{n_j} + \tau^2, \tag{6.4}\]

where \(\bar{y}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i y_{ij}\) is the study mean, \(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i \mathbf{x}_{ij}\) is the study-mean design vector, and \(\bar{s}_j^2 = n_j^{-1} \sum_i V_{ij}\) is the mean sampling variance. The marginal variance \(v_j\) combines within-study sampling uncertainty (\(\bar{s}_j^2 / n_j\)) and between-study heterogeneity (\(\tau^2\)).

The marginal parameter vector is \(\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}', \tau^2)' \in \mathbb{R}^{K+1}\).

6.2.2 Score Function

The study-level marginal log-likelihood is

\[ \ell_j(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\log(2\pi) + \log v_j + r_j^2 / v_j\right], \tag{6.5}\]

where \(r_j = \bar{y}_j - \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j' \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) is the marginal residual. Differentiating with respect to each parameter at the posterior mean \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}\) yields the likelihood score vector:

\[ \mathbf{s}_j = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial \ell_j}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} \\[4pt] \frac{\partial \ell_j}{\partial \tau^2} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{r_j}{v_j} \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \\[4pt] -\frac{1}{2v_j} + \frac{r_j^2}{2v_j^2} \end{pmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{K+1}. \tag{6.6}\]

The \(J \times (K+1)\) score matrix \(\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_J)'\) is computed in .marginal_sandwich(). In the study-level path (cluster-constant predictors), the computation is fully vectorized:

score_beta <- (r_j / v_j) * x_bar       # J x K
score_tau2 <- -1 / (2 * v_j) + r_j^2 / (2 * v_j^2)
S <- cbind(score_beta, score_tau2)       # J x (K+1)

6.2.3 Bread, Meat, and HC0 Sandwich

The posterior Hessian (bread) uses the positive-definite convention \(\mathbf{H} = -\partial^2 \log p(\boldsymbol{\phi} \mid \mathbf{y}) / \partial \boldsymbol{\phi} \partial \boldsymbol{\phi}'\) and decomposes as

\[ \mathbf{H}_{\text{post}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{lik}} + \mathbf{H}_{\text{prior}}, \tag{6.7}\]

where \(\mathbf{H}_{\text{lik}}\) is the negative second derivative of the likelihood and \(\mathbf{H}_{\text{prior}}\) captures prior curvature. The likelihood Hessian has blocks (computed in .build_marginal_hessian()):

\[ \begin{aligned} H_{\beta\beta} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'}{v_j}, & H_{\beta,\tau^2} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{r_j \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j}{v_j^2}, & H_{\tau^2,\tau^2} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[-\frac{1}{2v_j^2} + \frac{r_j^2}{v_j^3}\right]. \end{aligned} \tag{6.8}\]

The stabilized prior Hessian (.build_prior_hessian()) is diagonal: \(\operatorname{diag}(1/\sigma_\beta^2, \ldots, 1/\sigma_\beta^2, \,h_{\tau^2})\), where \(h_{\tau^2}\) is a numerical ridge stabilizer capped at \(10^6\) to prevent overflow when \(\hat{\tau}^2 \approx 0\).

The meat is the outer product of likelihood scores only:

\[ \mathbf{M} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j' = \mathbf{S}'\mathbf{S}. \tag{6.9}\]

Prior scores are excluded from the meat. This is the foundational principle of sandwich estimation: the meat measures data-driven between-cluster variability, which captures working-model misspecification. Including prior scores would contaminate this signal with data-external information, destroying the estimator’s robustness. The prior enters only through the bread, where it determines the location of the Bayesian center; its influence is \(O(1/J)\) relative to the likelihood contribution and vanishes asymptotically.

The HC0 sandwich estimator is

\[ \mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1} \, \mathbf{M} \, \mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1}. \tag{6.10}\]

The \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-block target is the upper-left \(K \times K\) submatrix of \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}\). In .marginal_sandwich(), this extraction is

V_sand <- H_inv %*% M %*% H_inv
V_beta <- V_sand[seq_len(K), seq_len(K), drop = FALSE]

6.2.4 Finite-Sample Bias of HC0

While \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}\) is consistent as \(J \to \infty\), it is downward-biased in finite samples:

\[ \mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}] < \mathrm{Var}_{\text{freq}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}), \tag{6.11}\]

because the residuals \(\hat{e}_j\) used in the meat understate the true errors—the fitted model absorbs some of the variation that should appear in the residuals. This bias is analogous to the well-known \(n/(n-p)\) correction in ordinary least squares but takes a matrix-valued form in the clustered setting. The CR2 correction of Bell & McCaffrey (2002), described in sec-cr2, addresses this bias.

6.3 Weight Matrices and the Woodbury Identity

6.3.1 The Working Covariance and Its Inverse

Under the CE working model (\(\rho_{\text{work}} = 0\)), the marginal covariance of \(\mathbf{y}_j\) is

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j = \bar{s}_j^2 \mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2 \mathbf{1}_{n_j} \mathbf{1}_{n_j}', \tag{6.12}\]

a scaled identity plus a rank-one matrix. The weight matrix \(\mathbf{W}_j = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\) is computed by the Woodbury matrix identity.

Applying the Woodbury matrix identity (the full derivation is given in sec-proof-woodbury) yields:

\[ \mathbf{W}_j = \frac{1}{\bar{s}_j^2}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \frac{\tau^2}{\bar{s}_j^2 \, c_j}\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j}, \tag{6.13}\]

where \(\mathbf{J}_{n_j} = \mathbf{1}_{n_j}\mathbf{1}_{n_j}'\) is the \(n_j \times n_j\) all-ones matrix and

\[ c_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + n_j \tau^2. \tag{6.14}\]

The intuition is that the rank-one structure of \(\tau^2\mathbf{J}_{n_j}\) allows the Woodbury identity to reduce the \(n_j \times n_j\) inversion to a scalar computation. The correction term \(-(\tau^2/(\bar{s}_j^2 c_j))\mathbf{J}_{n_j}\) downweights the common component shared across effect sizes within a study.

This is implemented in .compute_W_j(). For singletons (\(n_j = 1\)), the weight reduces to the scalar \(1/(\bar{s}_j^2 + \tau^2)\).

The Woodbury form reduces the computational cost of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\) from \(O(n_j^3)\) to \(O(n_j)\), and all downstream operations—score computation (\(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{r}_j\)), Hessian blocks (\(\mathbf{X}_j' \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{X}_j\))—benefit proportionally. For example, applying \(\mathbf{W}_j\) to a residual vector:

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{r}_j = \frac{\mathbf{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2} - \frac{\tau^2 \cdot \bar{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2 \cdot v_j}\,\mathbf{1}_{n_j}, \tag{6.15}\]

where \(\bar{r}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i r_{ij}\) and \(v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2\).

6.3.2 The Study-Level Path

When all predictors are cluster-constant (the common case), the observation-level computation collapses to a scalar path. The study-mean marginal model has variance \(v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2\), and the weight is the scalar \(w_j = 1/v_j\). In .compute_wls_bread(), this is the “study” level path:

v_j <- s_bar2 / n_j + tau2_hat
W_list[[j]] <- matrix(1 / v_j[j], 1L, 1L)
X_list[[j]] <- matrix(x_bar[j, ], nrow = 1L)

The study-level and observation-level paths produce identical results for cluster-constant designs but differ when within-study varying moderators are present (sec-varying-moderators).

6.3.3 CHE Weight Matrices

The CHE model adds within-study heterogeneity \(\omega^2\), giving marginal covariance

\[ \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{\text{CHE}} = (\bar{s}_j^2 + \omega^2)\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j} = a_j\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j}, \tag{6.16}\]

where the unified absorption parameter is

\[ a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2. \tag{6.17}\]

Setting \(\omega^2 = 0\) recovers the CE case. The Woodbury inverse is identical in form to Equation eq-woodbury-wj with \(\bar{s}_j^2\) replaced by \(a_j\):

\[ \mathbf{W}_j^{\text{CHE}} = \frac{1}{a_j}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \frac{\tau^2}{a_j\,c_j}\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j}, \quad c_j = a_j + n_j\tau^2. \tag{6.18}\]

In .compute_W_j(), the omega2_hat parameter (default 0) controls this substitution, implementing Decision D20.

6.3.4 Analytical \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\): The Rank-1 Woodbury Square Root

The CR2 symmetrization procedure (sec-cr2) requires both \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\) and \(\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}\). Because \(\mathbf{W}_j\) has compound-symmetric structure, its eigenvalues are \(\lambda_1 = 1/c_j\) (eigenvector \(\mathbf{1}_{n_j}/\sqrt{n_j}\)) and \(\lambda_2 = 1/a_j\) (multiplicity \(n_j - 1\)). Taking the positive square root of each eigenvalue and reassembling via the spectral theorem yields closed-form expressions (Decision D24). The full derivation is given in sec-proof-wsqrt.

\[ \boxed{ \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a_j}}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \frac{1}{n_j}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{c_j}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{a_j}}\right)\mathbf{J}_{n_j}. } \tag{6.19}\]

\[ \boxed{ \mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2} = \sqrt{a_j}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \frac{1}{n_j}\bigl(\sqrt{c_j} - \sqrt{a_j}\bigr)\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j}. } \tag{6.20}\]

The key insight is that the rank-1 structure of \(\mathbf{J}_{n_j}\) means \(\mathbf{W}_j\) has only two distinct eigenvalues, so both the square root and inverse square root remain in the same \(\alpha\mathbf{I} + \beta\mathbf{J}\) family—no eigendecomposition is needed at runtime. This analytical formula, implemented in .compute_W_j_sqrt(), reduces cost from \(O(n_j^3)\) to \(O(1)\) scalar operations plus \(O(n_j)\) array fills. The function falls back to eigendecomposition only when eigenvalues are non-positive (a numerical edge case).

6.4 The WLS Bread and Hat Matrix

6.4.1 Why the WLS Bread, Not the Posterior Hessian

Decision D15 mandates that the CR2 computation use the weighted-least-squares (WLS) bread

\[ \mathbf{Q} = \left(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\right)^{-1} \tag{6.21}\]

rather than the posterior Hessian \(\mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1}\). This distinction is critical. CR2 is a frequentist bias correction derived from the properties of the WLS hat matrix; it assumes the bread is the inverse of the weighted normal equations. The posterior Hessian includes prior precision, which would distort the hat matrix and invalidate the CR2 adjustment.

In the study-level path, Equation eq-wls-bread simplifies to

\[ \mathbf{Q} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j\,\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'}{v_j}\right)^{-1}, \tag{6.22}\]

where \(v_j = a_j / n_j + \tau^2\). In the observation-level path (within-study varying predictors), the full \(n_j \times n_j\) weight matrices \(\mathbf{W}_j\) are used.

The WLS bread is computed by .compute_wls_bread(), which returns \(\mathbf{Q}\), the per-study weight matrices \(\mathbf{W}_j\), design matrices \(\mathbf{X}_j\), and a level indicator (“study” or “obs”). For the CHE model, the omega2_hat argument flows through to .compute_W_j(), implementing the \(a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2\) substitution.

6.4.2 The Hat Matrix

The cluster-level hat matrix block for study \(j\) is

\[ \mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j, \tag{6.23}\]

an \(n_j \times n_j\) matrix (generally not symmetric). In the study-level path, this reduces to the scalar leverage \(h_j = \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'\,\mathbf{Q}\,\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j / v_j\). The hat matrix blocks are computed by .compute_hat_blocks(), which also tracks diagnostic quantities including the maximum leverage across studies.

The diagonal entries of \(\mathbf{H}_{jj}\) measure how much influence study \(j\) exerts on the fitted values. When \(\max_j h_j > 0.5\), the function emits a warning because the CR2 adjustment may be numerically unstable for high-leverage studies.

6.4.3 The Symmetrized Hat Matrix

For the CR2 eigendecomposition, we need a symmetric version of \(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}\). The symmetrization uses the weight square root from sec-wsqrt:

\[ \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}, \tag{6.24}\]

which is symmetric by construction. The matrix \(\mathbf{I} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}\) then has a well-defined matrix square root via eigendecomposition, which is the foundation of the CR2 adjustment.

6.5 CR2 Bias Correction

6.5.1 The Bias Equation

The HC0 sandwich Equation eq-hc0-sandwich uses residuals \(\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) in the meat. Because \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) is fitted to the data, the residuals systematically underestimate the true errors. To see the exact form of this bias, decompose the WLS residual.

The WLS estimator is \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{y}\), so the residual for study \(j\) is

\[ \hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\sum_{l=1}^{J}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l\mathbf{y}_l. \tag{6.25}\]

Writing \(\mathbf{y}_l = \mathbf{X}_l\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l\) and using \(\mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{X}_j\boldsymbol{\beta}\):

\[ \hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l=1}^{J} \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l = (\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l, \tag{6.26}\]

where \(\mathbf{H}_{jl} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l\) and \(\mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\) is the \(j\)-th diagonal block of the hat matrix.

Under between-study independence (\(\mathrm{Cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l) = \mathbf{0}\) for \(j \neq l\)), the expected outer product is

\[\begin{align} \mathrm{E}[\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j'] &= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})' + \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_l\,\mathbf{H}_{jl}'. \tag{3.B1} \end{align}\]

The first term dominates and is strictly smaller than \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\) (since \(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}\) is a contraction). The second (cross-study) term is \(O(1/J)\) and vanishes asymptotically. The net effect is that the HC0 meat is downward-biased:

\[ \mathrm{E}[\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j'] \neq \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j, \tag{6.27}\]

and the deficit grows with leverage.

The scalar corrections HC1 (\(J/(J-K)\)), HC2 (\((1-h_j)^{-1}\)), and HC3 (\((1-h_j)^{-2}\)) address this for univariate observations. For clustered data, the bias correction must be matrix-valued: each study \(j\) requires an \(n_j \times n_j\) adjustment that accounts for the full hat matrix block \(\mathbf{H}_{jj}\), not merely its trace or diagonal.

6.5.2 The CR2 Goal

The CR2 correction of Bell & McCaffrey (2002), adapted for meta-analysis by Tipton (2015), seeks adjustment matrices \(\mathbf{A}_j\) such that the sandwich variance estimator is unbiased:

\[ \mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}] = \text{Var}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \mathbf{Q}. \tag{6.28}\]

This aggregate target is achieved by choosing each \(\mathbf{A}_j\) to solve a per-study sufficient condition. Focusing on the dominant within-study contribution (the cross-study terms are \(O(1/J)\) and vanish under study independence), the design equation for \(\mathbf{A}_j\) is

\[ \mathbf{A}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})'\,\mathbf{A}_j' = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j. \tag{6.29}\]

6.5.3 Symmetrization via \(\mathbf{W}^{1/2}\)

A complication arises because \(\mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\) is generally not symmetric (the right multiplication by \(\mathbf{W}_j\) breaks symmetry whenever \(\mathbf{W}_j\) has off-diagonal elements). The matrix \(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}\) is therefore not symmetric, and a matrix square root is not uniquely defined without additional structure.

Bell & McCaffrey (2002) resolved this by conjugating with \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\). Define the symmetrized hat block

\[ \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}. \tag{6.30}\]

This is symmetric because it has the form \(\mathbf{B}\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{B}'\) with \(\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\mathbf{X}_j\). The relationship to the original hat block is a similarity transform:

\[ \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{H}_{jj}\,\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}, \tag{6.31}\]

so \(\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}\) and \(\mathbf{H}_{jj}\) have the same eigenvalues.

6.5.4 Derivation of \(\mathbf{A}_j\)

The derivation proceeds by conjugating the bias equation Equation eq-cr2-goal with \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\) to work in a symmetrized space, solving for the adjustment in that space, and back-transforming. The full step-by-step proof is given in sec-proof-cr2. The result is:

\[ \boxed{ \mathbf{A}_j = \mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}\,(\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj})^{-1/2}\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}. } \tag{6.32}\]

The three computational stages are: (1) form the symmetrized hat matrix \(\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}\) using the analytical \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\) from Equation eq-wsqrt-che; (2) eigendecompose \(\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}\) and take the inverse square root of each eigenvalue; (3) sandwich between \(\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}\) and \(\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\). In the implementation (.compute_cr2_adjustments()), eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{I} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}\) below \(10^{-10}\) are truncated for numerical stability, with a warning.

6.5.5 The Scalar Case

When \(n_j = 1\) or at the study level, all matrices reduce to scalars. The hat block becomes \(H_{jj} = h_j = \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'\mathbf{Q}\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \cdot w_j\), and the CR2 adjustment simplifies to

\[ A_j = (1 - h_j)^{-1/2}, \tag{6.33}\]

the familiar HC2-type correction (MacKinnon and White, 1985) applied at the cluster level.

This three-step procedure (symmetrize, eigendecompose, back-transform) is implemented in .compute_cr2_adjustments(). When a study’s leverage \(h_j\) exceeds 0.999, it is capped to prevent numerical overflow, with a warning.

6.5.6 The CR2 Sandwich

With the adjustment matrices in hand, the CR2-adjusted scores are

\[ \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{A}_j\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j, \tag{6.34}\]

and the CR2 sandwich variance estimator is

\[ \boxed{ \mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}} = \mathbf{Q}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j'\right)\mathbf{Q}. } \tag{6.35}\]

The bread is the WLS bread \(\mathbf{Q}\) (Equation eq-wls-bread), not the posterior Hessian. This is computed in .compute_vcr2(). In the study-level path, each adjusted score is a \(K\)-vector:

s_tilde_j <- x_bar[j, ] * w_j * a_j * r_j
M_CR2 <- M_CR2 + tcrossprod(s_tilde_j)

Decision D12, finalized in v0.5.0, mandates that \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\) (not \(c_\beta \cdot \mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}\)) is the CCC target. This is because CR2 provides study-specific, matrix-valued corrections that a scalar multiplier cannot replicate, particularly in designs with heterogeneous study sizes or unbalanced moderators.

The key properties of CR2 that distinguish it from simpler corrections are:

  1. Bias removal. \(\mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}] \approx \mathrm{Var}_{\text{freq}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})\) under the working model (Bell & McCaffrey, 2002), with the approximation becoming exact when cross-study hat matrix blocks are negligible.
  2. Matrix-valued adjustment. Each \(\mathbf{A}_j\) is an \(n_j \times n_j\) matrix, capturing the full influence structure within each cluster.
  3. Exact recovery. When all \(n_j = 1\) and leverage is uniform, CR2 reduces to the classical \(J/(J-K)\) correction.

6.6 Satterthwaite Degrees of Freedom

6.6.1 The Reference Distribution Problem

Even with the bias-reduced \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\), inference requires a reference distribution for the test statistic

\[ T_k = \frac{\hat{\beta}_k}{\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}(\hat{\beta}_k)} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_k}{\sqrt{[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}]_{kk}}}. \tag{6.36}\]

Under \(H_0: \beta_k = 0\), the numerator \(\hat{\beta}_k\) is approximately normal, but the denominator \(\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}\) is itself a random variable (it depends on the data through the residuals and variance component estimates). Using \(T_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\) treats \(\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}\) as if it were known, ignoring its sampling variability and leading to undercoverage when \(J\) is small. The simulation study sim3 confirmed this: CCC intervals using posterior quantiles (CCC-q, implicitly \(z\)-based) achieved only 91–94% coverage at the nominal 95% level when \(J \leq 20\), while CCC-\(t\) with Satterthwaite degrees of freedom achieved 94.5–95.3%.

The classical solution is to model \(T_k^2 \approx F(1, \nu_k)\) under \(H_0\), or equivalently \(T_k \sim t_{\nu_k}\), where the degrees of freedom \(\nu_k\) quantify how precisely \(\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}\) estimates the true standard error. Larger \(\nu_k\) means the variance estimate is more precise, producing a reference distribution closer to the normal; smaller \(\nu_k\) yields heavier tails and wider intervals.

6.6.2 The Satterthwaite Moment-Matching Principle

Satterthwaite (1946) proposed approximating the distribution of a positive quadratic form in normal variables by a scaled chi-squared distribution, with the scale and degrees of freedom determined by matching the first two moments.

Suppose \(\hat{V}\) is a variance estimator with \(\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}] = V\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}) = \sigma^2_V\). We seek \(c\) and \(\nu\) such that \(\hat{V} \approx c\,\chi^2_\nu\), which has \(\mathrm{E}[c\chi^2_\nu] = c\nu\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(c\chi^2_\nu) = 2c^2\nu\). Setting \(c\nu = V\) and \(2c^2\nu = \sigma^2_V\) and eliminating \(c\):

\[ \nu = \frac{2V^2}{\sigma^2_V} = \frac{2\,[\mathrm{E}(\hat{V})]^2}{\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V})}. \tag{6.37}\]

If \(\hat{V}\) is an equally-weighted average of \(m\) independent \(\chi^2_1\) variables (all with the same scale), the formula gives \(\nu = m\)—the exact result. When the weights are unequal, \(\nu < m\), reflecting the reduced effective sample size.

6.6.3 Derivation of \(\nu_k\) for the CR2 Estimator

We now derive the Satterthwaite degrees of freedom for the \(k\)-th diagonal element of \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\), following Bell & McCaffrey (2002) and the generalization by Imbens & Kolesár (2016).

Step 1: Express \(\hat{V}_k\) as a sum of squared linear forms.

Let \(\mathbf{c}_k \in \mathbb{R}^K\) be the \(k\)-th standard basis vector. The variance estimate for coefficient \(k\) is

\[ \hat{V}_k = \mathbf{c}_k'\,\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\,\mathbf{c}_k = \mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\left(\sum_{j=1}^J \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j'\right)\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{c}_k = \sum_{j=1}^J \bigl(\mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\bigr)^2. \tag{6.38}\]

Each term \(\mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\) is a scalar that depends on the \(j\)-th cluster’s adjusted score.

Step 2: Linearize in the errors.

Expanding the adjusted score \(\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{A}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\) and the residual \(\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l\):

\[ \mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{A}_j\left[(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l\right]. \]

This is a linear function of the full error vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = (\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_1', \ldots, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_J')'\). Therefore \(\hat{V}_k\) is a quadratic form in Gaussian errors, and the Satterthwaite approximation applies.

Step 3: Define the deterministic \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}\) vectors.

For each study \(j\) and coefficient \(k\), define the \(n_j\)-vector

\[ \mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{A}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{c}_k. \tag{6.39}\]

This vector is deterministic conditional on the plug-in variance components (\(\hat{\tau}^2\), \(\hat{\omega}^2\))—it depends on the design matrices \(\mathbf{X}_j\), the weights \(\mathbf{W}_j\), the bread \(\mathbf{Q}\), and the CR2 adjustments \(\mathbf{A}_j\), but not directly on the outcome vector \(\mathbf{y}\). The inner product \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l\) captures how the errors from study \(l\) contribute to the \(k\)-th variance estimate through study \(j\)’s adjusted score.

Step 4: Compute \(\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}_k)\) via the \(J \times J\) matrix \(\mathbf{P}_k\).

Because \(\hat{V}_k\) is a quadratic form in the Gaussian vector \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), its variance is determined by the trace of the square of an associated matrix. Define the symmetric \(J \times J\) matrix \(\mathbf{P}_k\) as follows.

The contribution of cluster \(j\)’s errors to \(\hat{V}_k\) (through both its own residual and its influence on other clusters’ residuals via the hat matrix) gives rise to the \((i,j)\) element of \(\mathbf{P}_k\).

Diagonal elements: The term \([\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj}\) measures the variance contribution of study \(j\) alone:

\[ [\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj} = \mathbf{g}_{jk}'\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,\mathbf{g}_{jk} - \mathbf{g}_{jk}'\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{g}_{jk}. \tag{6.40}\]

The first term is \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\mathbf{W}_j^{-1}\mathbf{g}_{jk}\) (since \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j = \mathbf{W}_j^{-1}\)), which is the variance of \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j\). The second term is a leverage correction that accounts for the fact that \(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}\) absorbs some of the signal from study \(j\).

Off-diagonal elements: The cross-cluster terms arise from the hat matrix coupling:

\[ [\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij} = -\mathbf{g}_{ik}'\,\mathbf{X}_i\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{g}_{jk}, \qquad i \neq j. \tag{6.41}\]

This can be rewritten efficiently using the \(K\)-vector \(\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}\):

\[ [\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij} = -(\mathbf{X}_i'\mathbf{g}_{ik})'\,\mathbf{Q}\,(\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}), \tag{6.42}\]

requiring only a \(K\)-dimensional inner product per pair \((i, j)\).

The matrix \(\mathbf{P}_k\) is symmetric by construction (the off-diagonal formula is symmetric in \(i\) and \(j\)).

Step 5: Apply the moment-matching formula.

Under normality, it can be shown that \(\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_k] = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k)\) and \(\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}_k) = 2\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)\) (Bell & McCaffrey, 2002, Lemma 1). Substituting into Equation eq-satt-general:

\[ \boxed{ \nu_k = \frac{[\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k)]^2}{\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)}. } \tag{6.43}\]

The traces are computed as:

\[ \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k) = \sum_{j=1}^J [\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj}, \qquad \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2) = \sum_{i=1}^J \sum_{j=1}^J [\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij}^2, \tag{6.44}\]

where the second equality uses \(\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}^2) = \|\mathbf{P}\|_F^2\) for symmetric matrices.

6.6.4 Connection to Imbens & Kolesár (2016)

Imbens & Kolesár (2016) generalized the Bell–McCaffrey framework to arbitrary linear contrasts \(\mathbf{c}'\boldsymbol{\beta}\). Their Theorem 1 shows that under normality of \(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}\), the random variable \(\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}} / \mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}]\) is exactly distributed as a weighted sum of independent \(\chi^2_1\) variables:

\[ \frac{\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}}{\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}]} \sim \sum_{i=1}^J \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_l \lambda_l}\,\chi^2_{1,i}, \]

where \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_J\) are the eigenvalues of \(\mathbf{P}_{\mathbf{c}}\). The Satterthwaite approximation \(\nu = (\sum \lambda_i)^2 / (\sum \lambda_i^2)\) matches the first two moments of this distribution exactly. This provides a rigorous theoretical justification for Equation eq-satt-df beyond the heuristic moment-matching argument.

6.6.5 Computational Implementation

The computation is implemented in .satterthwaite_df(). For efficiency, the function precomputes \(\mathbf{G}_j = \mathbf{A}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\) (an \(n_j \times K\) matrix) for each study, so that \(\mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{G}_j[\,,\,k]\). The computation proceeds as:

  1. Precompute \(\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'\) for each \(j\) (used in the off-diagonal terms).
  2. Precompute \(\mathbf{G}_j = \mathbf{A}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\) for each \(j\) (one matrix product).
  3. For each coefficient \(k\):
    1. Extract \(\mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{G}_j[\,,\,k]\) for all \(j\).
    2. Precompute \(\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}\) for all \(j\) (\(K\)-vectors).
    3. Build the \(J \times J\) matrix \(\mathbf{P}_k\) using Equation eq-pk-diag and Equation eq-pk-offdiag-efficient.
    4. Compute \(\nu_k = (\operatorname{tr}\mathbf{P}_k)^2 / \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)\).

The total cost per coefficient is \(O(J^2 K)\) for the off-diagonal elements.

6.6.6 Interpretation and Edge Cases

Per-coefficient degrees of freedom. Each \(\beta_k\) has its own \(\nu_k\), reflecting the fact that different coefficients may be estimated with different effective sample sizes. A coefficient that correlates strongly with high-leverage studies will have smaller \(\nu_k\).

Upper bound. When all studies contribute equally (balanced design, equal \(n_j\)), \(\mathbf{P}_k\) approaches a scalar multiple of \(\mathbf{I}_J\), and \(\nu_k \to J\).

Deterministic property. The \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}\) vectors (Equation eq-gjk) depend only on \(\mathbf{X}\), \(\mathbf{W}\), \(\mathbf{Q}\), and \(\mathbf{A}\)—not on the data \(\mathbf{y}\). Therefore \(\nu_k\) is a fixed property of the design and variance components, not a random variable.

Small \(\mathrm{df}_k\). When a few studies have high leverage (e.g., one study with many effect sizes and a unique moderator value), those studies dominate the variance estimate. The \(t\) distribution has heavier tails, producing wider intervals. Tipton (2015) reported that with \(J = 28\), a single coefficient could have \(\mathrm{df}_k = 2.8\), resulting in a critical value of \(t_{2.8, 0.025} = 3.28\) versus \(z_{0.025} = 1.96\)—a 67% wider interval.

Fallback logic. When the Satterthwaite computation fails for a coefficient (e.g., \(\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2) \approx 0\) or numerical overflow), the function returns NA and the caller substitutes the naive degrees of freedom \(\mathrm{df}_{\text{naive}} = \max(J - K, 1)\). When \(\mathrm{df}_k < 1\), the value is also replaced with \(\mathrm{df}_{\text{naive}}\). When \(\mathrm{df}_k < 4\), a warning is emitted following Tipton (2015)’s recommendation that inference is unreliable in this regime. The df_source vector in the bm_sandwich object records whether each coefficient used the Satterthwaite or naive value.

6.7 Within-Study Varying Moderators

When the design matrix \(\mathbf{X}\) contains predictors that vary within studies (e.g., outcome type, measurement instrument), the study-level aggregation \(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j = n_j^{-1}\sum_i \mathbf{x}_{ij}\) is no longer sufficient. The scores must be computed at the observation level.

Auto-detection. The function .is_cluster_constant() in data-prep.R checks whether all predictors are constant within each study during data preparation. The result is stored in bm_data$cluster_constant and queried by sandwich.bm_fit() to select the computation path.

Observation-level scores. When within-study varying predictors are detected, the \(\boldsymbol{\beta}\)-score for study \(j\) is computed as

\[ \mathbf{s}_j^{(\beta)} = \mathbf{X}_j'\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\,\mathbf{r}_j = \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} \left[\frac{r_{ij}}{\bar{s}_j^2} - \frac{\tau^2\,\bar{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2\,v_j}\right] \mathbf{x}_{ij}, \tag{6.45}\]

using the Woodbury form of \(\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\mathbf{r}_j\) (Equation eq-woodbury-residual). The \(\tau^2\)-score remains a study-level quantity because the marginal likelihood for \(\tau^2\) depends only on \(\bar{r}_j\).

Impact on dimensions. In the observation-level path, \(\mathbf{W}_j\) is \(n_j \times n_j\) (not \(1 \times 1\)), \(\mathbf{X}_j\) is \(n_j \times K\) (not \(1 \times K\)), and the hat matrix block \(\mathbf{H}_{jj}\) is \(n_j \times n_j\). All downstream CR2 computations—the symmetrized hat matrix, the \(\mathbf{A}_j\) adjustment, and the Satterthwaite \(\mathbf{g}_{jk}\) vectors—operate at observation level without modification to their mathematical form, only to their dimensions.

In .compute_wls_bread(), the observation-level path is

X_list[[j]] <- X_obs[idx, , drop = FALSE]    # n_j x K
W_list[[j]] <- .compute_W_j(s_bar2[j], tau2_hat, nj)
XtWX <- XtWX + crossprod(X_list[[j]], W_list[[j]] %*% X_list[[j]])

6.8 The CHE Overlay Pattern

6.8.1 The Substitution Principle

The central insight of the v0.6.0 implementation is that the CHE model requires no new mathematical machinery for sandwich estimation. The CHE marginal covariance (Equation eq-che-sigma) has the same algebraic structure as the CE covariance (Equation eq-sigma-j) with the substitution \(\bar{s}_j^2 \to a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2\). Every formula in sec-weight-matrices through sec-satterthwaite carries over with this single parameter change.

The plug-in estimator for \(\omega^2\) is

\[ \hat{\omega}^2 = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S} \omega^{(s)2}, \tag{6.46}\]

the posterior mean of \(\omega^2\) (Decision D20). This is the mean of the squared draws, not the square of the mean draw. The distinction matters because \(\mathrm{E}[\omega^2] \neq (\mathrm{E}[\omega])^2\) by Jensen’s inequality; using \((\bar{\omega})^2\) would underestimate the true posterior mean of the variance component. This convention is consistent with the CE treatment: \(\hat{\tau}^2 = S^{-1}\sum_s \tau^{(s)2}\).

6.8.2 Implementation: A Single Parameter

In .compute_W_j(), the CHE overlay is a single line:

a_j <- s_bar2_j + omega2_hat

In .compute_wls_bread(), it flows through as the omega2_hat argument:

bread_info <- .compute_wls_bread(bm_data, tau2_hat, K, J,
                                  omega2_hat = omega2_hat)

All downstream functions—.compute_hat_blocks(), .compute_cr2_adjustments(), .compute_vcr2(), .satterthwaite_df()—are reused without modification. The CHE dispatch in sandwich.bm_fit() delegates to .che_sandwich(), which calls the same shared infrastructure.

6.8.3 Gate 1: CE–CHE Equivalence

When \(\omega^2 = 0\), the CHE weights reduce to CE weights at machine precision. This is verified by the Gate 1 test: fitting a CHE model to data generated with \(\omega^2 = 0\) and confirming that all sandwich outputs (V_CR2, Satterthwaite df) match the CE result to within floating-point tolerance. This gate ensures that the CHE overlay introduces no regressions for the CE special case.

6.8.4 Singleton Non-Identifiability

When a study has \(n_j = 1\), the within-study variance \(\omega^2\) and between-study variance \(\tau^2\) are not separately identified from the data; separation relies entirely on the prior. Decision D22 mandates warnings in .che_sandwich():

  • If all studies are singletons (\(n_j = 1\) for all \(j\)): a warning that \(\tau^2\) and \(\omega^2\) are “not separately identified.”
  • If more than 50% of studies are singletons: a warning that the parameters may be “weakly identified.”

These warnings do not block computation but alert the user that CR2 benchmarks from a frequentist multilevel analysis may diverge from the Bayesian results in this regime.

6.8.5 The Plug-In Parity Test (Decision D25)

To validate the linear-algebra pathway independently of MCMC noise, Decision D25 specifies a plug-in parity test: inject the REML estimates of \(\tau^2\) and \(\omega^2\) (from metafor::rma.mv) into the bayesRVE sandwich machinery and verify that \(\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\) matches the clubSandwich::vcovCR(type = "CR2") result to within \(10^{-6}\). This test isolates the CR2 computation from posterior estimation, confirming that the shared CE/CHE infrastructure produces numerically identical results to the reference implementation. The plug-in parity test passes for both CE and CHE models across all test scenarios in the v0.6.0 validation suite.

# Sandwich Variance Estimation {#sec-sandwich}

This chapter develops the complete theory of cluster-robust sandwich variance estimation as implemented in `sandwich.R`, the mathematical heart of bayesRVE.
The sandwich estimator provides the frequentist-valid variance target that the Cholesky Calibration Correction (CCC, @sec-ccc) uses to recalibrate Bayesian posterior draws.
We cover the HC0 estimator, the CR2 bias correction of @bell2002, Satterthwaite degrees of freedom following @tipton2015, and the CHE overlay pattern that unifies the correlated-effects (CE) and correlated-hierarchical-effects (CHE) models under a single computational framework.

The presentation is self-contained: a reader familiar with weighted least squares and basic matrix algebra can follow this chapter without reference to other parts of the report.


## Why the Full-Joint Sandwich Is the Wrong Target {#sec-wrong-target}

### The Full Parameter Vector

In the CE conditional model (@sec-ce-model), the full parameter vector is

$$
\boldsymbol{\psi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}', \theta_1, \ldots, \theta_J)' \in \mathbb{R}^{K+J},
$$ {#eq-full-param}

where $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ collects $K$ fixed-effect coefficients and $\theta_j$ are study-specific random intercepts.
A naive approach would compute the full $(K+J) \times (K+J)$ sandwich:

$$
\mathbf{V}_{\text{sand}}^{\text{full}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}^{-1} \, \mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} \, \mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}^{-1},
$$ {#eq-full-joint-sandwich}

where $\mathbf{H}_{\text{full}}$ is the posterior Hessian and $\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j'$ is the outer-product meat.
This approach fails for three distinct reasons.

**The $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-block collapses.**
In the conditional model, $\theta_j$ acts as a study-specific intercept that absorbs nearly all between-study variation.
The $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-scores $\partial \ell_j / \partial \boldsymbol{\beta}$ become nearly constant across studies, so the meat's $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-block---which measures between-study heterogeneity of scores---is near zero.
The sandwich would dramatically understate the true sampling variance of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$.

**Structural rank deficiency.**
The meat $\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j'$ is a sum of $J$ rank-one matrices, so $\operatorname{rank}(\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}}) \leq J$.
Since the parameter dimension is $d = K + J > J$ (for $K \geq 1$), $\mathbf{M}_{\text{full}}$ is structurally singular.
A Cholesky factorization of the full sandwich, as CCC requires, is therefore impossible.
This reflects the classical incidental-parameters problem [@neyman1948]: with $J$ nuisance parameters estimated from $J$ clusters, the meat cannot span the full parameter space.

**Cubic computational cost.**
Inverting a $(K+J) \times (K+J)$ Hessian costs $O((K+J)^3)$, which becomes prohibitive as $J$ grows into the hundreds.


### The Blockwise Strategy

The correct approach, codified in Decision D2, is a blockwise hybrid target.
The key insight is that $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ and $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ play fundamentally different roles: $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the inferential target; $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is a vector of nuisance parameters.

For the $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-block, we integrate out $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ to obtain a marginal model in which the between-study heterogeneity captured by $\theta_j$ is absorbed into the marginal variance $v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2$.
The marginal sandwich then measures the correct quantity: the between-study variability of $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-scores under the marginal likelihood.

For the $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-block, we condition on $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ and exploit the fact that, given $\boldsymbol{\beta}$, the $\theta_j$ are conditionally independent.
The conditional sandwich reduces to $J$ scalar computations.

The CCC target is therefore block-diagonal:

$$
\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}} = \begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\beta)} & \mathbf{0} \\
\mathbf{0} & \mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\theta)}
\end{pmatrix},
$$ {#eq-blockwise-target}

where $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\beta)}$ is a $K \times K$ matrix from the marginal sandwich and $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CCC}}^{(\theta)}$ is a diagonal $J \times J$ matrix from the conditional sandwich.
The cross-block is set to zero by design (not estimation), reflecting the blockwise application of CCC (@sec-ccc).
This reduces the Cholesky cost from $O((K+J)^3)$ to $O(K^3 + J)$.

In `sandwich.R`, the marginal sandwich is computed by `.marginal_sandwich()` and the conditional sandwich by `.conditional_sandwich()`.
The CHE model, where $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is analytically integrated out, requires only the marginal component (via `.che_sandwich()`).
The final object is assembled by `.assemble_bm_sandwich()`.


## The Marginal Score and HC0 Sandwich {#sec-hc0}

### The CE Marginal Model

Integrating out $\theta_j$ from the CE conditional model (@sec-ce-model) yields the marginal distribution of study means:

$$
\bar{y}_j \mid \boldsymbol{\beta}, \tau^2 \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j' \boldsymbol{\beta},\; v_j\right),
\quad v_j = \frac{\bar{s}_j^2}{n_j} + \tau^2,
$$ {#eq-ce-marginal-dist}

where $\bar{y}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i y_{ij}$ is the study mean, $\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i \mathbf{x}_{ij}$ is the study-mean design vector, and $\bar{s}_j^2 = n_j^{-1} \sum_i V_{ij}$ is the mean sampling variance.
The marginal variance $v_j$ combines within-study sampling uncertainty ($\bar{s}_j^2 / n_j$) and between-study heterogeneity ($\tau^2$).

The marginal parameter vector is $\boldsymbol{\phi} = (\boldsymbol{\beta}', \tau^2)' \in \mathbb{R}^{K+1}$.


### Score Function

The study-level marginal log-likelihood is

$$
\ell_j(\boldsymbol{\phi}) = -\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left[\log(2\pi) + \log v_j + r_j^2 / v_j\right],
$$ {#eq-marginal-loglik}

where $r_j = \bar{y}_j - \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j' \hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is the marginal residual.
Differentiating with respect to each parameter at the posterior mean $\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ yields the likelihood score vector:

$$
\mathbf{s}_j = \begin{pmatrix}
\frac{\partial \ell_j}{\partial \boldsymbol{\beta}} \\[4pt]
\frac{\partial \ell_j}{\partial \tau^2}
\end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix}
\frac{r_j}{v_j} \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \\[4pt]
-\frac{1}{2v_j} + \frac{r_j^2}{2v_j^2}
\end{pmatrix}
\in \mathbb{R}^{K+1}.
$$ {#eq-ce-score}

The $J \times (K+1)$ score matrix $\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_J)'$ is computed in `.marginal_sandwich()`.
In the study-level path (cluster-constant predictors), the computation is fully vectorized:

```r
score_beta <- (r_j / v_j) * x_bar       # J x K
score_tau2 <- -1 / (2 * v_j) + r_j^2 / (2 * v_j^2)
S <- cbind(score_beta, score_tau2)       # J x (K+1)
```


### Bread, Meat, and HC0 Sandwich

The posterior Hessian (bread) uses the positive-definite convention $\mathbf{H} = -\partial^2 \log p(\boldsymbol{\phi} \mid \mathbf{y}) / \partial \boldsymbol{\phi} \partial \boldsymbol{\phi}'$ and decomposes as

$$
\mathbf{H}_{\text{post}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{lik}} + \mathbf{H}_{\text{prior}},
$$ {#eq-hessian-decomp}

where $\mathbf{H}_{\text{lik}}$ is the negative second derivative of the likelihood and $\mathbf{H}_{\text{prior}}$ captures prior curvature.
The likelihood Hessian has blocks (computed in `.build_marginal_hessian()`):

$$
\begin{aligned}
H_{\beta\beta} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'}{v_j}, &
H_{\beta,\tau^2} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{r_j \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j}{v_j^2}, &
H_{\tau^2,\tau^2} &= \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[-\frac{1}{2v_j^2} + \frac{r_j^2}{v_j^3}\right].
\end{aligned}
$$ {#eq-hessian-blocks}

The stabilized prior Hessian (`.build_prior_hessian()`) is diagonal: $\operatorname{diag}(1/\sigma_\beta^2, \ldots, 1/\sigma_\beta^2, \,h_{\tau^2})$, where $h_{\tau^2}$ is a numerical ridge stabilizer capped at $10^6$ to prevent overflow when $\hat{\tau}^2 \approx 0$.

The meat is the outer product of likelihood scores only:

$$
\mathbf{M} = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{s}_j \mathbf{s}_j' = \mathbf{S}'\mathbf{S}.
$$ {#eq-meat}

Prior scores are excluded from the meat.
This is the foundational principle of sandwich estimation: the meat measures data-driven between-cluster variability, which captures working-model misspecification.
Including prior scores would contaminate this signal with data-external information, destroying the estimator's robustness.
The prior enters only through the bread, where it determines the location of the Bayesian center; its influence is $O(1/J)$ relative to the likelihood contribution and vanishes asymptotically.

The HC0 sandwich estimator is

$$
\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}} = \mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1} \, \mathbf{M} \, \mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1}.
$$ {#eq-hc0-sandwich}

The $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-block target is the upper-left $K \times K$ submatrix of $\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}$.
In `.marginal_sandwich()`, this extraction is

```r
V_sand <- H_inv %*% M %*% H_inv
V_beta <- V_sand[seq_len(K), seq_len(K), drop = FALSE]
```


### Finite-Sample Bias of HC0

While $\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}$ is consistent as $J \to \infty$, it is downward-biased in finite samples:

$$
\mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}] < \mathrm{Var}_{\text{freq}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}),
$$ {#eq-hc0-bias}

because the residuals $\hat{e}_j$ used in the meat understate the true errors---the fitted model absorbs some of the variation that should appear in the residuals.
This bias is analogous to the well-known $n/(n-p)$ correction in ordinary least squares but takes a matrix-valued form in the clustered setting.
The CR2 correction of @bell2002, described in @sec-cr2, addresses this bias.


## Weight Matrices and the Woodbury Identity {#sec-weight-matrices}

### The Working Covariance and Its Inverse

Under the CE working model ($\rho_{\text{work}} = 0$), the marginal covariance of $\mathbf{y}_j$ is

$$
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j = \bar{s}_j^2 \mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2 \mathbf{1}_{n_j} \mathbf{1}_{n_j}',
$$ {#eq-sigma-j}

a scaled identity plus a rank-one matrix.
The weight matrix $\mathbf{W}_j = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}$ is computed by the Woodbury matrix identity.

Applying the Woodbury matrix identity (the full derivation is given in @sec-proof-woodbury) yields:

$$
\mathbf{W}_j = \frac{1}{\bar{s}_j^2}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \frac{\tau^2}{\bar{s}_j^2 \, c_j}\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j},
$$ {#eq-woodbury-wj}

where $\mathbf{J}_{n_j} = \mathbf{1}_{n_j}\mathbf{1}_{n_j}'$ is the $n_j \times n_j$ all-ones matrix and

$$
c_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + n_j \tau^2.
$$ {#eq-cj-def}

The intuition is that the rank-one structure of $\tau^2\mathbf{J}_{n_j}$ allows the Woodbury identity to reduce the $n_j \times n_j$ inversion to a scalar computation.
The correction term $-(\tau^2/(\bar{s}_j^2 c_j))\mathbf{J}_{n_j}$ downweights the common component shared across effect sizes within a study.

This is implemented in `.compute_W_j()`.
For singletons ($n_j = 1$), the weight reduces to the scalar $1/(\bar{s}_j^2 + \tau^2)$.

The Woodbury form reduces the computational cost of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}$ from $O(n_j^3)$ to $O(n_j)$, and all downstream operations---score computation ($\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{r}_j$), Hessian blocks ($\mathbf{X}_j' \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{X}_j$)---benefit proportionally.
For example, applying $\mathbf{W}_j$ to a residual vector:

$$
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1} \mathbf{r}_j = \frac{\mathbf{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2} - \frac{\tau^2 \cdot \bar{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2 \cdot v_j}\,\mathbf{1}_{n_j},
$$ {#eq-woodbury-residual}

where $\bar{r}_j = n_j^{-1} \sum_i r_{ij}$ and $v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2$.


### The Study-Level Path

When all predictors are cluster-constant (the common case), the observation-level computation collapses to a scalar path.
The study-mean marginal model has variance $v_j = \bar{s}_j^2 / n_j + \tau^2$, and the weight is the scalar $w_j = 1/v_j$.
In `.compute_wls_bread()`, this is the "study" level path:

```r
v_j <- s_bar2 / n_j + tau2_hat
W_list[[j]] <- matrix(1 / v_j[j], 1L, 1L)
X_list[[j]] <- matrix(x_bar[j, ], nrow = 1L)
```

The study-level and observation-level paths produce identical results for cluster-constant designs but differ when within-study varying moderators are present (@sec-varying-moderators).


### CHE Weight Matrices

The CHE model adds within-study heterogeneity $\omega^2$, giving marginal covariance

$$
\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{\text{CHE}} = (\bar{s}_j^2 + \omega^2)\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j} = a_j\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \tau^2\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j},
$$ {#eq-che-sigma}

where the unified absorption parameter is

$$
a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2.
$$ {#eq-aj-def}

Setting $\omega^2 = 0$ recovers the CE case.
The Woodbury inverse is identical in form to @eq-woodbury-wj with $\bar{s}_j^2$ replaced by $a_j$:

$$
\mathbf{W}_j^{\text{CHE}} = \frac{1}{a_j}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \frac{\tau^2}{a_j\,c_j}\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j},
\quad c_j = a_j + n_j\tau^2.
$$ {#eq-che-woodbury}

In `.compute_W_j()`, the `omega2_hat` parameter (default 0) controls this substitution, implementing Decision D20.


### Analytical $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$: The Rank-1 Woodbury Square Root {#sec-wsqrt}

The CR2 symmetrization procedure (@sec-cr2) requires both $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$ and $\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}$.
Because $\mathbf{W}_j$ has compound-symmetric structure, its eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1/c_j$ (eigenvector $\mathbf{1}_{n_j}/\sqrt{n_j}$) and $\lambda_2 = 1/a_j$ (multiplicity $n_j - 1$).
Taking the positive square root of each eigenvalue and reassembling via the spectral theorem yields closed-form expressions (Decision D24). The full derivation is given in @sec-proof-wsqrt.

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a_j}}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \frac{1}{n_j}\!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{c_j}} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{a_j}}\right)\mathbf{J}_{n_j}.
}
$$ {#eq-wsqrt-che}

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2} = \sqrt{a_j}\,\mathbf{I}_{n_j} + \frac{1}{n_j}\bigl(\sqrt{c_j} - \sqrt{a_j}\bigr)\,\mathbf{J}_{n_j}.
}
$$ {#eq-wsqrtinv-che}

The key insight is that the rank-1 structure of $\mathbf{J}_{n_j}$ means $\mathbf{W}_j$ has only two distinct eigenvalues, so both the square root and inverse square root remain in the same $\alpha\mathbf{I} + \beta\mathbf{J}$ family---no eigendecomposition is needed at runtime.
This analytical formula, implemented in `.compute_W_j_sqrt()`, reduces cost from $O(n_j^3)$ to $O(1)$ scalar operations plus $O(n_j)$ array fills.
The function falls back to eigendecomposition only when eigenvalues are non-positive (a numerical edge case).


## The WLS Bread and Hat Matrix {#sec-wls-bread}

### Why the WLS Bread, Not the Posterior Hessian

Decision D15 mandates that the CR2 computation use the weighted-least-squares (WLS) bread

$$
\mathbf{Q} = \left(\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X}\right)^{-1} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\right)^{-1}
$$ {#eq-wls-bread}

rather than the posterior Hessian $\mathbf{H}_{\text{post}}^{-1}$.
This distinction is critical.
CR2 is a frequentist bias correction derived from the properties of the WLS hat matrix; it assumes the bread is the inverse of the weighted normal equations.
The posterior Hessian includes prior precision, which would distort the hat matrix and invalidate the CR2 adjustment.

In the study-level path, @eq-wls-bread simplifies to

$$
\mathbf{Q} = \left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \frac{\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j\,\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'}{v_j}\right)^{-1},
$$ {#eq-wls-bread-study}

where $v_j = a_j / n_j + \tau^2$.
In the observation-level path (within-study varying predictors), the full $n_j \times n_j$ weight matrices $\mathbf{W}_j$ are used.

The WLS bread is computed by `.compute_wls_bread()`, which returns $\mathbf{Q}$, the per-study weight matrices $\mathbf{W}_j$, design matrices $\mathbf{X}_j$, and a level indicator ("study" or "obs").
For the CHE model, the `omega2_hat` argument flows through to `.compute_W_j()`, implementing the $a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2$ substitution.


### The Hat Matrix

The cluster-level hat matrix block for study $j$ is

$$
\mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j,
$$ {#eq-hat-block}

an $n_j \times n_j$ matrix (generally not symmetric).
In the study-level path, this reduces to the scalar leverage $h_j = \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'\,\mathbf{Q}\,\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j / v_j$.
The hat matrix blocks are computed by `.compute_hat_blocks()`, which also tracks diagnostic quantities including the maximum leverage across studies.

The diagonal entries of $\mathbf{H}_{jj}$ measure how much influence study $j$ exerts on the fitted values.
When $\max_j h_j > 0.5$, the function emits a warning because the CR2 adjustment may be numerically unstable for high-leverage studies.

### The Symmetrized Hat Matrix

For the CR2 eigendecomposition, we need a symmetric version of $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}$.
The symmetrization uses the weight square root from @sec-wsqrt:

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2},
$$ {#eq-hat-sym}

which is symmetric by construction.
The matrix $\mathbf{I} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}$ then has a well-defined matrix square root via eigendecomposition, which is the foundation of the CR2 adjustment.


## CR2 Bias Correction {#sec-cr2}

### The Bias Equation

The HC0 sandwich @eq-hc0-sandwich uses residuals $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ in the meat.
Because $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ is fitted to the data, the residuals systematically underestimate the true errors.
To see the exact form of this bias, decompose the WLS residual.

The WLS estimator is $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\,\mathbf{y}$, so the residual for study $j$ is

$$
\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \mathbf{y}_j - \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\sum_{l=1}^{J}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l\mathbf{y}_l.
$$ {#eq-residual-expand}

Writing $\mathbf{y}_l = \mathbf{X}_l\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l$ and using $\mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}'\mathbf{W}\mathbf{X}\boldsymbol{\beta} = \mathbf{X}_j\boldsymbol{\beta}$:

$$
\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l=1}^{J} \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l = (\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l,
$$ {#eq-residual-decompose}

where $\mathbf{H}_{jl} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_l'\mathbf{W}_l$ and $\mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j$ is the $j$-th diagonal block of the hat matrix.

Under between-study independence ($\mathrm{Cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l) = \mathbf{0}$ for $j \neq l$), the expected outer product is

\begin{align}
\mathrm{E}[\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j']
&= (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})' + \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_l\,\mathbf{H}_{jl}'. \tag{3.B1}
\end{align}

The first term dominates and is strictly smaller than $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j$ (since $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}$ is a contraction).
The second (cross-study) term is $O(1/J)$ and vanishes asymptotically.
The net effect is that the HC0 meat is downward-biased:

$$
\mathrm{E}[\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j'] \neq \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j,
$$ {#eq-residual-bias}

and the deficit grows with leverage.

The scalar corrections HC1 ($J/(J-K)$), HC2 ($(1-h_j)^{-1}$), and HC3 ($(1-h_j)^{-2}$) address this for univariate observations.
For clustered data, the bias correction must be matrix-valued: each study $j$ requires an $n_j \times n_j$ adjustment that accounts for the full hat matrix block $\mathbf{H}_{jj}$, not merely its trace or diagonal.


### The CR2 Goal

The CR2 correction of @bell2002, adapted for meta-analysis by @tipton2015, seeks adjustment matrices $\mathbf{A}_j$ such that the sandwich variance estimator is unbiased:

$$
\mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}] = \text{Var}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \mathbf{Q}.
$$ {#eq-cr2-unbiased}

This aggregate target is achieved by choosing each $\mathbf{A}_j$ to solve a per-study sufficient condition. Focusing on the dominant within-study contribution (the cross-study terms are $O(1/J)$ and vanish under study independence), the design equation for $\mathbf{A}_j$ is

$$
\mathbf{A}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})'\,\mathbf{A}_j' = \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j.
$$ {#eq-cr2-goal}


### Symmetrization via $\mathbf{W}^{1/2}$

A complication arises because $\mathbf{H}_{jj} = \mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j$ is generally **not symmetric** (the right multiplication by $\mathbf{W}_j$ breaks symmetry whenever $\mathbf{W}_j$ has off-diagonal elements).
The matrix $\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj}$ is therefore not symmetric, and a matrix square root is not uniquely defined without additional structure.

@bell2002 resolved this by conjugating with $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$.
Define the symmetrized hat block

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}.
$$ {#eq-hat-sym-cr2}

This is symmetric because it has the form $\mathbf{B}\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{B}'$ with $\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\mathbf{X}_j$.
The relationship to the original hat block is a similarity transform:

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj} = \mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}\,\mathbf{H}_{jj}\,\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2},
$$ {#eq-hat-similarity}

so $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}$ and $\mathbf{H}_{jj}$ have the same eigenvalues.


### Derivation of $\mathbf{A}_j$

The derivation proceeds by conjugating the bias equation @eq-cr2-goal with $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$ to work in a symmetrized space, solving for the adjustment in that space, and back-transforming. The full step-by-step proof is given in @sec-proof-cr2. The result is:

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{A}_j = \mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}\,(\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj})^{-1/2}\,\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}.
}
$$ {#eq-aj-backtransform}

The three computational stages are: (1) form the symmetrized hat matrix $\tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}$ using the analytical $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$ from @eq-wsqrt-che; (2) eigendecompose $\mathbf{I}_{n_j} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}$ and take the inverse square root of each eigenvalue; (3) sandwich between $\mathbf{W}_j^{-1/2}$ and $\mathbf{W}_j^{1/2}$.
In the implementation (`.compute_cr2_adjustments()`), eigenvalues of $\mathbf{I} - \tilde{\mathbf{H}}_{jj}$ below $10^{-10}$ are truncated for numerical stability, with a warning.


### The Scalar Case

When $n_j = 1$ or at the study level, all matrices reduce to scalars.
The hat block becomes $H_{jj} = h_j = \bar{\mathbf{x}}_j'\mathbf{Q}\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j \cdot w_j$, and the CR2 adjustment simplifies to

$$
A_j = (1 - h_j)^{-1/2},
$$ {#eq-aj-scalar}

the familiar HC2-type correction (MacKinnon and White, 1985) applied at the cluster level.

This three-step procedure (symmetrize, eigendecompose, back-transform) is implemented in `.compute_cr2_adjustments()`.
When a study's leverage $h_j$ exceeds 0.999, it is capped to prevent numerical overflow, with a warning.


### The CR2 Sandwich

With the adjustment matrices in hand, the CR2-adjusted scores are

$$
\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{A}_j\,\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j,
$$ {#eq-cr2-score}

and the CR2 sandwich variance estimator is

$$
\boxed{
\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}} = \mathbf{Q}\left(\sum_{j=1}^{J} \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j'\right)\mathbf{Q}.
}
$$ {#eq-vcr2}

The bread is the WLS bread $\mathbf{Q}$ (@eq-wls-bread), not the posterior Hessian.
This is computed in `.compute_vcr2()`.
In the study-level path, each adjusted score is a $K$-vector:

```r
s_tilde_j <- x_bar[j, ] * w_j * a_j * r_j
M_CR2 <- M_CR2 + tcrossprod(s_tilde_j)
```

Decision D12, finalized in v0.5.0, mandates that $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}$ (not $c_\beta \cdot \mathbf{V}_{\text{HC0}}$) is the CCC target.
This is because CR2 provides study-specific, matrix-valued corrections that a scalar multiplier cannot replicate, particularly in designs with heterogeneous study sizes or unbalanced moderators.

The key properties of CR2 that distinguish it from simpler corrections are:

1. **Bias removal.** $\mathrm{E}[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}] \approx \mathrm{Var}_{\text{freq}}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}})$ under the working model [@bell2002], with the approximation becoming exact when cross-study hat matrix blocks are negligible.
2. **Matrix-valued adjustment.** Each $\mathbf{A}_j$ is an $n_j \times n_j$ matrix, capturing the full influence structure within each cluster.
3. **Exact recovery.** When all $n_j = 1$ and leverage is uniform, CR2 reduces to the classical $J/(J-K)$ correction.


## Satterthwaite Degrees of Freedom {#sec-satterthwaite}

### The Reference Distribution Problem

Even with the bias-reduced $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}$, inference requires a reference distribution for the test statistic

$$
T_k = \frac{\hat{\beta}_k}{\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}(\hat{\beta}_k)} = \frac{\hat{\beta}_k}{\sqrt{[\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}]_{kk}}}.
$$ {#eq-test-stat}

Under $H_0: \beta_k = 0$, the numerator $\hat{\beta}_k$ is approximately normal, but the denominator $\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}$ is itself a random variable (it depends on the data through the residuals and variance component estimates).
Using $T_k \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ treats $\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}$ as if it were known, ignoring its sampling variability and leading to undercoverage when $J$ is small.
The simulation study sim3 confirmed this: CCC intervals using posterior quantiles (CCC-q, implicitly $z$-based) achieved only 91--94\% coverage at the nominal 95\% level when $J \leq 20$, while CCC-$t$ with Satterthwaite degrees of freedom achieved 94.5--95.3\%.

The classical solution is to model $T_k^2 \approx F(1, \nu_k)$ under $H_0$, or equivalently $T_k \sim t_{\nu_k}$, where the degrees of freedom $\nu_k$ quantify how precisely $\operatorname{SE}_{\text{CR2}}$ estimates the true standard error.
Larger $\nu_k$ means the variance estimate is more precise, producing a reference distribution closer to the normal; smaller $\nu_k$ yields heavier tails and wider intervals.

### The Satterthwaite Moment-Matching Principle

@satterthwaite1946 proposed approximating the distribution of a positive quadratic form in normal variables by a scaled chi-squared distribution, with the scale and degrees of freedom determined by matching the first two moments.

Suppose $\hat{V}$ is a variance estimator with $\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}] = V$ and $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}) = \sigma^2_V$.
We seek $c$ and $\nu$ such that $\hat{V} \approx c\,\chi^2_\nu$, which has $\mathrm{E}[c\chi^2_\nu] = c\nu$ and $\mathrm{Var}(c\chi^2_\nu) = 2c^2\nu$.
Setting $c\nu = V$ and $2c^2\nu = \sigma^2_V$ and eliminating $c$:

$$
\nu = \frac{2V^2}{\sigma^2_V} = \frac{2\,[\mathrm{E}(\hat{V})]^2}{\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V})}.
$$ {#eq-satt-general}

If $\hat{V}$ is an equally-weighted average of $m$ independent $\chi^2_1$ variables (all with the same scale), the formula gives $\nu = m$---the exact result.
When the weights are unequal, $\nu < m$, reflecting the reduced effective sample size.


### Derivation of $\nu_k$ for the CR2 Estimator

We now derive the Satterthwaite degrees of freedom for the $k$-th diagonal element of $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}$, following @bell2002 and the generalization by @imbens2016.

**Step 1: Express $\hat{V}_k$ as a sum of squared linear forms.**

Let $\mathbf{c}_k \in \mathbb{R}^K$ be the $k$-th standard basis vector.
The variance estimate for coefficient $k$ is

$$
\hat{V}_k = \mathbf{c}_k'\,\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}\,\mathbf{c}_k
= \mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\left(\sum_{j=1}^J \tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j'\right)\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{c}_k
= \sum_{j=1}^J \bigl(\mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j\bigr)^2.
$$ {#eq-vk-quadratic}

Each term $\mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j$ is a scalar that depends on the $j$-th cluster's adjusted score.

**Step 2: Linearize in the errors.**

Expanding the adjusted score $\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{A}_j\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j$ and the residual $\hat{\mathbf{e}}_j = (\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l$:

$$
\mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\tilde{\mathbf{s}}_j = \mathbf{c}_k'\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{A}_j\left[(\mathbf{I} - \mathbf{H}_{jj})\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j - \sum_{l \neq j}\mathbf{H}_{jl}\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l\right].
$$

This is a linear function of the full error vector $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = (\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_1', \ldots, \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_J')'$.
Therefore $\hat{V}_k$ is a quadratic form in Gaussian errors, and the Satterthwaite approximation applies.

**Step 3: Define the deterministic $\mathbf{g}_{jk}$ vectors.**

For each study $j$ and coefficient $k$, define the $n_j$-vector

$$
\mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{A}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{c}_k.
$$ {#eq-gjk}

This vector is deterministic conditional on the plug-in variance components ($\hat{\tau}^2$, $\hat{\omega}^2$)---it depends on the design matrices $\mathbf{X}_j$, the weights $\mathbf{W}_j$, the bread $\mathbf{Q}$, and the CR2 adjustments $\mathbf{A}_j$, but not directly on the outcome vector $\mathbf{y}$.
The inner product $\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_l$ captures how the errors from study $l$ contribute to the $k$-th variance estimate through study $j$'s adjusted score.

**Step 4: Compute $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}_k)$ via the $J \times J$ matrix $\mathbf{P}_k$.**

Because $\hat{V}_k$ is a quadratic form in the Gaussian vector $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, its variance is determined by the trace of the square of an associated matrix.
Define the symmetric $J \times J$ matrix $\mathbf{P}_k$ as follows.

The contribution of cluster $j$'s errors to $\hat{V}_k$ (through both its own residual and its influence on other clusters' residuals via the hat matrix) gives rise to the $(i,j)$ element of $\mathbf{P}_k$.

*Diagonal elements:* The term $[\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj}$ measures the variance contribution of study $j$ alone:

$$
[\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj} = \mathbf{g}_{jk}'\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j\,\mathbf{g}_{jk} - \mathbf{g}_{jk}'\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{g}_{jk}.
$$ {#eq-pk-diag}

The first term is $\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\mathbf{W}_j^{-1}\mathbf{g}_{jk}$ (since $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j = \mathbf{W}_j^{-1}$), which is the variance of $\mathbf{g}_{jk}'\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}_j$.
The second term is a leverage correction that accounts for the fact that $\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}$ absorbs some of the signal from study $j$.

*Off-diagonal elements:* The cross-cluster terms arise from the hat matrix coupling:

$$
[\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij} = -\mathbf{g}_{ik}'\,\mathbf{X}_i\,\mathbf{Q}\,\mathbf{X}_j'\,\mathbf{g}_{jk}, \qquad i \neq j.
$$ {#eq-pk-offdiag}

This can be rewritten efficiently using the $K$-vector $\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}$:

$$
[\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij} = -(\mathbf{X}_i'\mathbf{g}_{ik})'\,\mathbf{Q}\,(\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}),
$$ {#eq-pk-offdiag-efficient}

requiring only a $K$-dimensional inner product per pair $(i, j)$.

The matrix $\mathbf{P}_k$ is symmetric by construction (the off-diagonal formula is symmetric in $i$ and $j$).

**Step 5: Apply the moment-matching formula.**

Under normality, it can be shown that $\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_k] = \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k)$ and $\mathrm{Var}(\hat{V}_k) = 2\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)$ [@bell2002, Lemma 1].
Substituting into @eq-satt-general:

$$
\boxed{
\nu_k = \frac{[\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k)]^2}{\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)}.
}
$$ {#eq-satt-df}

The traces are computed as:

$$
\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k) = \sum_{j=1}^J [\mathbf{P}_k]_{jj}, \qquad
\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2) = \sum_{i=1}^J \sum_{j=1}^J [\mathbf{P}_k]_{ij}^2,
$$ {#eq-traces}

where the second equality uses $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}^2) = \|\mathbf{P}\|_F^2$ for symmetric matrices.


### Connection to @imbens2016

@imbens2016 generalized the Bell--McCaffrey framework to arbitrary linear contrasts $\mathbf{c}'\boldsymbol{\beta}$.
Their Theorem 1 shows that under normality of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, the random variable $\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}} / \mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}]$ is exactly distributed as a weighted sum of independent $\chi^2_1$ variables:

$$
\frac{\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}}{\mathrm{E}[\hat{V}_{\mathbf{c}}]} \sim \sum_{i=1}^J \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_l \lambda_l}\,\chi^2_{1,i},
$$

where $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_J$ are the eigenvalues of $\mathbf{P}_{\mathbf{c}}$.
The Satterthwaite approximation $\nu = (\sum \lambda_i)^2 / (\sum \lambda_i^2)$ matches the first two moments of this distribution exactly.
This provides a rigorous theoretical justification for @eq-satt-df beyond the heuristic moment-matching argument.


### Computational Implementation

The computation is implemented in `.satterthwaite_df()`.
For efficiency, the function precomputes $\mathbf{G}_j = \mathbf{A}_j'\,\mathbf{W}_j\,\mathbf{X}_j\,\mathbf{Q}$ (an $n_j \times K$ matrix) for each study, so that $\mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{G}_j[\,,\,k]$.
The computation proceeds as:

1. Precompute $\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{X}_j'$ for each $j$ (used in the off-diagonal terms).
2. Precompute $\mathbf{G}_j = \mathbf{A}_j'\mathbf{W}_j\mathbf{X}_j\mathbf{Q}$ for each $j$ (one matrix product).
3. For each coefficient $k$:
   a. Extract $\mathbf{g}_{jk} = \mathbf{G}_j[\,,\,k]$ for all $j$.
   b. Precompute $\mathbf{X}_j'\mathbf{g}_{jk}$ for all $j$ ($K$-vectors).
   c. Build the $J \times J$ matrix $\mathbf{P}_k$ using @eq-pk-diag and @eq-pk-offdiag-efficient.
   d. Compute $\nu_k = (\operatorname{tr}\mathbf{P}_k)^2 / \operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2)$.

The total cost per coefficient is $O(J^2 K)$ for the off-diagonal elements.


### Interpretation and Edge Cases

**Per-coefficient degrees of freedom.**
Each $\beta_k$ has its own $\nu_k$, reflecting the fact that different coefficients may be estimated with different effective sample sizes.
A coefficient that correlates strongly with high-leverage studies will have smaller $\nu_k$.

**Upper bound.** When all studies contribute equally (balanced design, equal $n_j$), $\mathbf{P}_k$ approaches a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{I}_J$, and $\nu_k \to J$.

**Deterministic property.** The $\mathbf{g}_{jk}$ vectors (@eq-gjk) depend only on $\mathbf{X}$, $\mathbf{W}$, $\mathbf{Q}$, and $\mathbf{A}$---not on the data $\mathbf{y}$.
Therefore $\nu_k$ is a fixed property of the design and variance components, not a random variable.

**Small $\mathrm{df}_k$.**
When a few studies have high leverage (e.g., one study with many effect sizes and a unique moderator value), those studies dominate the variance estimate.
The $t$ distribution has heavier tails, producing wider intervals.
@tipton2015 reported that with $J = 28$, a single coefficient could have $\mathrm{df}_k = 2.8$, resulting in a critical value of $t_{2.8, 0.025} = 3.28$ versus $z_{0.025} = 1.96$---a 67\% wider interval.

**Fallback logic.**
When the Satterthwaite computation fails for a coefficient (e.g., $\operatorname{tr}(\mathbf{P}_k^2) \approx 0$ or numerical overflow), the function returns `NA` and the caller substitutes the naive degrees of freedom $\mathrm{df}_{\text{naive}} = \max(J - K, 1)$.
When $\mathrm{df}_k < 1$, the value is also replaced with $\mathrm{df}_{\text{naive}}$.
When $\mathrm{df}_k < 4$, a warning is emitted following @tipton2015's recommendation that inference is unreliable in this regime.
The `df_source` vector in the `bm_sandwich` object records whether each coefficient used the Satterthwaite or naive value.


## Within-Study Varying Moderators {#sec-varying-moderators}

When the design matrix $\mathbf{X}$ contains predictors that vary within studies (e.g., outcome type, measurement instrument), the study-level aggregation $\bar{\mathbf{x}}_j = n_j^{-1}\sum_i \mathbf{x}_{ij}$ is no longer sufficient.
The scores must be computed at the observation level.

**Auto-detection.**
The function `.is_cluster_constant()` in `data-prep.R` checks whether all predictors are constant within each study during data preparation.
The result is stored in `bm_data$cluster_constant` and queried by `sandwich.bm_fit()` to select the computation path.

**Observation-level scores.**
When within-study varying predictors are detected, the $\boldsymbol{\beta}$-score for study $j$ is computed as

$$
\mathbf{s}_j^{(\beta)} = \mathbf{X}_j'\,\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\,\mathbf{r}_j
= \sum_{i=1}^{n_j} \left[\frac{r_{ij}}{\bar{s}_j^2} - \frac{\tau^2\,\bar{r}_j}{\bar{s}_j^2\,v_j}\right] \mathbf{x}_{ij},
$$ {#eq-obs-level-score}

using the Woodbury form of $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_j^{-1}\mathbf{r}_j$ (@eq-woodbury-residual).
The $\tau^2$-score remains a study-level quantity because the marginal likelihood for $\tau^2$ depends only on $\bar{r}_j$.

**Impact on dimensions.**
In the observation-level path, $\mathbf{W}_j$ is $n_j \times n_j$ (not $1 \times 1$), $\mathbf{X}_j$ is $n_j \times K$ (not $1 \times K$), and the hat matrix block $\mathbf{H}_{jj}$ is $n_j \times n_j$.
All downstream CR2 computations---the symmetrized hat matrix, the $\mathbf{A}_j$ adjustment, and the Satterthwaite $\mathbf{g}_{jk}$ vectors---operate at observation level without modification to their mathematical form, only to their dimensions.

In `.compute_wls_bread()`, the observation-level path is

```r
X_list[[j]] <- X_obs[idx, , drop = FALSE]    # n_j x K
W_list[[j]] <- .compute_W_j(s_bar2[j], tau2_hat, nj)
XtWX <- XtWX + crossprod(X_list[[j]], W_list[[j]] %*% X_list[[j]])
```


## The CHE Overlay Pattern {#sec-che-overlay}

### The Substitution Principle

The central insight of the v0.6.0 implementation is that the CHE model requires no new mathematical machinery for sandwich estimation.
The CHE marginal covariance (@eq-che-sigma) has the same algebraic structure as the CE covariance (@eq-sigma-j) with the substitution $\bar{s}_j^2 \to a_j = \bar{s}_j^2 + \hat{\omega}^2$.
Every formula in @sec-weight-matrices through @sec-satterthwaite carries over with this single parameter change.

The plug-in estimator for $\omega^2$ is

$$
\hat{\omega}^2 = \frac{1}{S}\sum_{s=1}^{S} \omega^{(s)2},
$$ {#eq-omega2-hat}

the posterior mean of $\omega^2$ (Decision D20).
This is the mean of the squared draws, not the square of the mean draw.
The distinction matters because $\mathrm{E}[\omega^2] \neq (\mathrm{E}[\omega])^2$ by Jensen's inequality; using $(\bar{\omega})^2$ would underestimate the true posterior mean of the variance component.
This convention is consistent with the CE treatment: $\hat{\tau}^2 = S^{-1}\sum_s \tau^{(s)2}$.


### Implementation: A Single Parameter

In `.compute_W_j()`, the CHE overlay is a single line:

```r
a_j <- s_bar2_j + omega2_hat
```

In `.compute_wls_bread()`, it flows through as the `omega2_hat` argument:

```r
bread_info <- .compute_wls_bread(bm_data, tau2_hat, K, J,
                                  omega2_hat = omega2_hat)
```

All downstream functions---`.compute_hat_blocks()`, `.compute_cr2_adjustments()`, `.compute_vcr2()`, `.satterthwaite_df()`---are reused without modification.
The CHE dispatch in `sandwich.bm_fit()` delegates to `.che_sandwich()`, which calls the same shared infrastructure.


### Gate 1: CE--CHE Equivalence

When $\omega^2 = 0$, the CHE weights reduce to CE weights at machine precision.
This is verified by the Gate 1 test: fitting a CHE model to data generated with $\omega^2 = 0$ and confirming that all sandwich outputs (V_CR2, Satterthwaite df) match the CE result to within floating-point tolerance.
This gate ensures that the CHE overlay introduces no regressions for the CE special case.


### Singleton Non-Identifiability

When a study has $n_j = 1$, the within-study variance $\omega^2$ and between-study variance $\tau^2$ are not separately identified from the data; separation relies entirely on the prior.
Decision D22 mandates warnings in `.che_sandwich()`:

- If **all** studies are singletons ($n_j = 1$ for all $j$): a warning that $\tau^2$ and $\omega^2$ are "not separately identified."
- If **more than 50\%** of studies are singletons: a warning that the parameters may be "weakly identified."

These warnings do not block computation but alert the user that CR2 benchmarks from a frequentist multilevel analysis may diverge from the Bayesian results in this regime.


### The Plug-In Parity Test (Decision D25)

To validate the linear-algebra pathway independently of MCMC noise, Decision D25 specifies a plug-in parity test: inject the REML estimates of $\tau^2$ and $\omega^2$ (from `metafor::rma.mv`) into the bayesRVE sandwich machinery and verify that $\mathbf{V}_{\text{CR2}}$ matches the `clubSandwich::vcovCR(type = "CR2")` result to within $10^{-6}$.
This test isolates the CR2 computation from posterior estimation, confirming that the shared CE/CHE infrastructure produces numerically identical results to the reference implementation.
The plug-in parity test passes for both CE and CHE models across all test scenarios in the v0.6.0 validation suite.
