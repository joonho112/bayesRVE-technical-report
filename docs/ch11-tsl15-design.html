13  Real-Data Validation: Experimental Design

This chapter documents the experimental design of the real-data validation study used to evaluate bayesRVE’s Three-Layer Calibration against established frequentist robust variance estimation methods. The validation uses a well-characterized meta-analytic dataset that has served as a benchmark in the RVE literature, enabling direct comparison against published results.

13.1 The Tanner-Smith & Lipsey (2015) Dataset

13.1.1 Overview

The validation dataset comes from the meta-analysis by Tanner-Smith & Lipsey (2015), which synthesized evidence on the effectiveness of brief alcohol interventions for adolescents and young adults. This dataset was subsequently reanalyzed by Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022) as a primary showcase for robust variance estimation methods in meta-analysis with dependent effect sizes, making it an ideal benchmark for evaluating bayesRVE.

13.1.2 Data Dimensions

After applying the inclusion filters from Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022)—removing observations with missing values on percoll, attrition_all, or permale—the analytic sample comprises:

Dimension Value
Total effect sizes (\(N\)) 1,198
Independent studies (\(J\)) 107
Effect size metric Standardized mean difference (Hedges’ \(g\))
Sampling variances Reported or estimated via standard formulas
Effect sizes per study 1 to 45 (median approximately 5)

A note on study counts: the original Tanner-Smith & Lipsey (2015) publication reports a larger sample. The \(J = 107\) figure reflects the analytic subset after the Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022) filtering criteria are applied and studies with missing covariate data are excluded. In the model design matrix, some sources report \(J = 117\) unique study clusters because certain studies contribute effect sizes across multiple dependent variable categories, creating additional effective clusters in the cross-classified structure.

13.1.3 Dependency Structure

The dataset exhibits substantial within-study dependence: individual studies contribute between 1 and 45 effect sizes, arising because a single intervention trial can measure multiple outcomes (e.g., frequency of use, quantity of use, peak consumption) at multiple follow-up time points. This dependence structure makes robust variance estimation essential for valid statistical inference, as standard methods that assume independence would understate uncertainty.

13.1.4 Rationale for Dataset Selection

The Tanner-Smith & Lipsey (2015) dataset was selected for the following reasons:

  1. Large number of studies. With \(J = 107\) independent studies, the dataset falls well within bayesRVE’s operating envelope (\(J_{\min} = \max(8, 2K + 2)\); see sec-envelope) for all moderator specifications considered, ensuring that CR2 corrections and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are well-identified.

  2. Heterogeneous cluster sizes. The range of 1 to 45 effect sizes per study exercises the sandwich estimator across a variety of cluster configurations, from singletons to large clusters.

  3. Established benchmark. Published results from Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022) using robumeta and clubSandwich provide a ground truth against which bayesRVE output can be directly compared.

  4. Substantive moderators. The dataset includes both categorical moderators (dependent variable category, control type) and continuous covariates, enabling validation of bayesRVE’s CCC machinery under realistic meta-regression specifications with \(K > 1\) predictors.

13.2 Model Specifications

Two moderator specifications are fit to the dataset, each using a cell-means parameterization (no intercept term, no reference category). This parameterization directly estimates the conditional mean effect size for each category level, facilitating interpretable comparisons across all groups.

13.2.1 DV Category Model

The DV Category model estimates mean effect sizes for six outcome categories, adjusted for five continuous covariates:

\[ g_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{6} \beta_k \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{DV}_{ij} = k] + \beta_7 \cdot \text{postwks\_c}_{ij} + \beta_8 \cdot \text{postwks\_long}_{ij} + \beta_9 \cdot \text{percoll\_c}_{ij} + \beta_{10} \cdot \text{permale\_c}_{ij} + \beta_{11} \cdot \text{attrition\_c}_{ij} + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij} \]

The six DV categories are:

  1. BAC (blood alcohol concentration)
  2. Combined measures
  3. Frequency of heavy use
  4. Frequency of use
  5. Peak consumption
  6. Quantity of use

This yields \(K = 11\) total regression coefficients (6 category indicators + 5 covariates).

13.2.2 Control Type Model

The Control Type model replaces the DV category indicators with four control-type indicators, using the same five continuous covariates:

\[ g_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{4} \gamma_k \cdot \mathbb{1}[\text{Ctype}_{ij} = k] + \gamma_5 \cdot \text{postwks\_c}_{ij} + \gamma_6 \cdot \text{postwks\_long}_{ij} + \gamma_7 \cdot \text{percoll\_c}_{ij} + \gamma_8 \cdot \text{permale\_c}_{ij} + \gamma_9 \cdot \text{attrition\_c}_{ij} + u_j + \varepsilon_{ij} \]

This yields \(K = 9\) total regression coefficients (4 control-type indicators + 5 covariates).

13.2.3 Covariate Definitions

All covariates are centered to improve MCMC sampling efficiency and interpretability:

Covariate Definition Centering
postwks_c Post-treatment follow-up (weeks), capped at 26 Centered at 12 weeks
postwks_long Indicator for follow-up exceeding 26 weeks Binary (0/1)
percoll_c Proportion of college-age participants Centered at 1.0
permale_c Proportion male in sample Centered at 0.5
attrition_c Overall attrition rate Centered at sample median

13.2.4 Random Effects Structure

Each model specification is fit under two random-effects structures:

  • CE (Correlated Effects): A single between-study random effect \(u_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\), inducing within-study correlation \(\rho = \tau^2/(\bar{s}_j^2 + \tau^2)\) structurally through the compound-symmetric covariance. In bayesRVE, \(\rho_{\text{work}} = 0\) during MCMC (D5); \(\rho\) enters only at the sandwich stage.

  • CHE (Correlated-Hierarchical Effects): Two random effects—a between-study component \(u_j \sim N(0, \tau^2)\) and a within-study component \(w_{ij} \sim N(0, \omega^2)\)—so that \(\text{Var}(g_{ij}) = v_{ij} + \omega^2 + \tau^2\).

In R formula syntax:

# CE model
bm_fit(es | se(V) ~ 0 + dv_cat + postwks_c + postwks_long +
       percoll_c + permale_c + attrition_c,
       data = TSL15, study = ~ studyid, model = "ce")

# CHE model
bm_fit(es | se(V) ~ 0 + dv_cat + postwks_c + postwks_long +
       percoll_c + permale_c + attrition_c,
       data = TSL15, study = ~ studyid, model = "che")

This yields \(2 \times 2 = 4\) bayesRVE model fits per moderator specification (CE/CHE crossed with DV/Control Type), plus the three frequentist baselines per specification, for a total of 14 fitted models.

13.3 Analysis Settings

13.3.1 MCMC Configuration

All bayesRVE models use the following MCMC settings:

Parameter Value Rationale
Chains 4 Standard for convergence assessment via \(\hat{R}\) and split-\(\hat{R}\)
Warmup iterations 2,000 Sufficient for NUTS adaptation (step size, mass matrix)
Sampling iterations 2,000 Yields \(S = 8{,}000\) total posterior draws
Random seed 20260324 Exact reproducibility across reruns
adapt_delta 0.95 Conservative target acceptance rate for meta-analysis models
max_treedepth 12 Standard upper bound on leapfrog trajectory length

The total of \(S = 8{,}000\) posterior draws provides adequate effective sample sizes for all parameters of interest given the relatively low dimensionality of the models (\(K \leq 11\) regression coefficients plus 1–2 variance components).

13.3.2 Sandwich and Calibration Settings

Parameter Value Rationale
Working correlation (\(\rho\)) 0.6 Recommended by Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022) for social science meta-analyses; sandwich estimator is consistent regardless of \(\rho\) choice
Small-sample correction CR2 Default (Decision D12); bias-reducing leverage adjustment
Degrees of freedom Satterthwaite Default (Decision D13); accounts for estimation uncertainty in variance components
Covariance imputation metafor::impute_covariance_matrix() with smooth_vi = TRUE (Viechtbauer, 2010) Constructs \(V\) matrix assuming equicorrelation within studies at \(\rho = 0.6\)

13.3.3 Prior Distributions

Parameter Prior Rationale
\(\beta_k\) (regression coefficients) \(N(0, 10)\) Weakly informative for standardized mean differences; prior SD of 10 is effectively flat on the SMD scale
\(\tau\) (between-study SD) Half-Cauchy\((0, 0.5)\) Standard weakly informative prior for heterogeneity in social science; allows substantial heterogeneity while mildly regularizing
\(\omega\) (within-study SD, CHE only) Half-Cauchy\((0, 0.5)\) Same specification as \(\tau\); symmetric treatment of variance components 
# CE prior specification
prior_ce <- bm_prior(beta = normal(0, 10), tau = half_cauchy(0, 0.5))

# CHE prior specification
prior_che <- bm_prior(beta = normal(0, 10),
                      tau = half_cauchy(0, 0.5),
                      omega = half_cauchy(0, 0.5))

The choice of Half-Cauchy(0, 0.5) for variance component priors follows the recommendations of Gelman (2006) and Polson & Scott (2012) for hierarchical models. The scale parameter of 0.5 is appropriate for standardized effect sizes where heterogeneity SDs rarely exceed 0.5 in most substantive domains, while the heavy tails of the Cauchy distribution allow the data to dominate when heterogeneity is larger.

13.4 Comparator Estimators

The validation compares five estimators that span the frequentist and Bayesian approaches to meta-analysis with dependent effect sizes. Each estimator pair is chosen to isolate specific sources of variation in the comparison.

13.4.1 Estimator Summary

Estimator Package Model Variance Estimation df Method Role
CE robumeta robumeta::robu CE working model CR2 (Tipton, 2015) Satterthwaite Published baseline
CE metafor metafor::rma.mv CE (\(\tau^2\) only) CR2 via clubSandwich Satterthwaite Fair CE comparison
CHE metafor metafor::rma.mv CHE (\(\tau^2 + \omega^2\)) CR2 via clubSandwich Satterthwaite Fair CHE comparison
bayesRVE CE bayesRVE::bm_fit CE (\(\tau^2\) only) CCC with CR2 target Satterthwaite This package (CE)
bayesRVE CHE bayesRVE::bm_fit CHE (\(\tau^2 + \omega^2\)) CCC with CR2 target Satterthwaite This package (CHE)

13.4.2 Estimator Descriptions

CE robumeta. The robumeta::robu function implements the correlated-effects working model of Hedges et al. (2010), computing study-level aggregated effect sizes under an assumed equicorrelation \(\rho\), then applying CR2 small-sample corrections following Tipton (2015). This estimator serves as the published baseline, replicating the exact results reported in Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022). Because robumeta performs study-mean aggregation internally, its point estimates and standard errors may differ slightly from observation-level estimators.

CE metafor. The metafor::rma.mv function (Viechtbauer, 2010) fits a single-\(\tau^2\) random-effects model at the observation level using REML, with the imputed covariance matrix \(V\) incorporating the working correlation \(\rho = 0.6\). CR2 standard errors and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are obtained via the clubSandwich package. This estimator provides a fair comparison target for bayesRVE CE because both operate on the same data structure (observation-level, single random effect) and apply the same CR2 + Satterthwaite inference framework.

CHE metafor. The metafor::rma.mv function with a two-level random structure (~ 1 | studyid / esid) fits the correlated-hierarchical effects model, estimating both \(\tau^2\) (between-study) and \(\omega^2\) (within-study) via REML. CR2 corrections and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are again obtained via clubSandwich. This estimator is the fair comparison target for bayesRVE CHE.

bayesRVE CE. The bayesRVE::bm_fit function with model = "ce" fits a Bayesian CE model via Stan (Carpenter et al., 2017) (NUTS sampler), estimating posterior distributions for all regression coefficients and \(\tau\). The Cholesky Calibration Correction (CCC) maps posterior draws to have the CR2 sandwich covariance as their dispersion target. Satterthwaite degrees of freedom define the reference distribution for inference (Three-Layer Calibration: L1 = posterior mean, L2 = CR2 sandwich, L3 = Satterthwaite \(t\)).

bayesRVE CHE. The bayesRVE::bm_fit function with model = "che" extends the CE model with a within-study variance component \(\omega^2\). The CHE CCC computation uses \(\hat{\omega}^2 = \text{mean}(\omega^{(s)2})\) (Decision D20) to construct the CR2 sandwich with Woodbury-based \(W^{1/2}\) weights (Decision D24). This estimator is the primary new capability introduced in v0.6.0.

13.4.3 Comparison Logic

The five-estimator design enables the following structured comparisons:

  1. CE robumeta vs. CE metafor: Quantifies the effect of study-mean aggregation (robumeta) versus observation-level modeling (metafor) within the CE framework. Differences in point estimates arise from the distinct weighting schemes.

  2. CE metafor vs. bayesRVE CE: The primary validation comparison for CE models. Both use observation-level data with a single random effect. Differences reflect the Bayesian-vs-REML estimation and the CCC calibration procedure. If CCC is working correctly, the CCC SD should approximate the CR2 SE.

  3. CHE metafor vs. bayesRVE CHE: The primary validation comparison for CHE models, introduced in v0.6.0. Both use two random effects. Agreement validates that the CHE extension of CCC (omega-aware weights, Woodbury \(W^{1/2}\)) produces calibrated inference.

  4. CE vs. CHE (within estimator): Reveals the effect of modeling within-study heterogeneity. In the CE model, \(\tau^2\) absorbs \(\omega^2\), producing larger between-study variance estimates. The CHE model decomposes total heterogeneity, generally yielding similar or slightly different standard errors depending on the relative magnitudes of \(\tau^2\) and \(\omega^2\).

13.5 Reproducibility and Decision Rules

13.5.1 Single-Script Reproducibility

The complete analysis—from data preparation through all 14 model fits to summary tables and figures—is contained in a single R script:

dev/log/201_real-data-v060/00_full_analysis.R

This script loads the raw dataset, applies all filters and transformations, fits all frequentist baselines and bayesRVE models, computes all comparison tables, and saves results to disk. No manual intervention or intermediate files are required. Given the fixed random seed (SEED = 20260324), all MCMC results are exactly reproducible on the same hardware and software configuration.

13.5.2 Pre-Specified Decision Rules

All model specifications, MCMC settings, prior distributions, and evaluation criteria were fixed before running the bayesRVE models. Specifically:

  • The two moderator specifications (DV Category, Control Type) replicate Pustejovsky & Tipton (2022) exactly.
  • The covariate centering scheme follows the original publication.
  • Prior distributions and MCMC settings were chosen based on general recommendations in the literature, not tuned to the TSL15 data.
  • No post-hoc model selection, predictor selection, or prior adjustment was performed.

13.5.3 Gate 3 Criterion

The primary success criterion for the real-data validation is:

\[ R_{\text{SE},k} = \frac{\text{CCC SD}_k}{\text{CR2 SE}_k} \in [0.9, 1.1] \quad \text{for all DV category coefficients } k = 1, \ldots, 6 \]

This criterion is evaluated separately for CE and CHE models:

  • CE: CCC SD from bayesRVE CE vs. CR2 SE from metafor CE (via clubSandwich)
  • CHE: CCC SD from bayesRVE CHE vs. CR2 SE from metafor CHE (via clubSandwich)

Gate 3 is defined exclusively for the six DV category coefficients (\(k = 1, \ldots, 6\)), which serve as intercepts in the DV-moderator specification and each draw information from a large number of contributing studies. Covariate coefficients (e.g., percoll_c, permale_c) are reported separately in sec-mechanics-covariates and are not subject to the Gate 3 acceptance band; their CCC/CR2 ratios may fall outside [0.9, 1.1] due to lower effective cluster counts and Satterthwaite df.

13.5.4 Rationale for the [0.9, 1.1] Band

The 10% tolerance band around unity accommodates two sources of discrepancy between the Bayesian CCC standard deviations and the frequentist CR2 standard errors:

  1. Variance-component plug-in discrepancy (primary). The CCC uses the Bayesian posterior mean of variance components (\(\tau^2\), \(\omega^2\)) as plug-in estimates for the weight matrix \(W\) and hence the bread matrix (\(X'WX\)), while the frequentist CR2 uses REML estimates. Because the posterior mean and REML estimate differ due to prior influence, the two methods construct different \(V_{\text{CR2}}\) targets. By Theorem 2, CCC achieves its own \(V_{\text{CR2}}\) exactly; the observed ratio \(\neq 1\) reflects two different targets, not a calibration failure. This typically contributes 1–5% discrepancy.

  2. MCMC sampling noise (secondary). Even with \(S = 8{,}000\) posterior draws, the sample covariance of the calibrated draws has finite Monte Carlo error. For well-behaved posteriors, this contributes approximately 1–2% variation and is reducible by increasing \(S\).

A ratio of exactly 1.0 would indicate perfect calibration; the [0.9, 1.1] band reflects the practical precision achievable given these two sources of variation. This tolerance was established a priori based on experience from the simulation studies (sim3, 24 conditions \(\times\) 500 replications), where CCC-\(t\) coverage achieved 94.5–95.3% nominal 95% coverage with SE ratios in this range.

13.5.5 Supplementary Diagnostics

Beyond the primary Gate 3 criterion, the following diagnostics are examined:

  • Shrinkage ratio \(R_k\). The ratio of CCC SD to raw posterior SD, quantifying how much the calibration correction adjusts each coefficient’s uncertainty. Values below 1.0 indicate CCC shrinkage (the raw posterior is overestimating uncertainty relative to the sandwich target); values above 1.0 indicate CCC inflation.

  • Satterthwaite df agreement. The Satterthwaite degrees of freedom from bayesRVE vs. those from clubSandwich, verifying that the independent implementation (Decision D11) produces consistent results.

  • Point estimate concordance. Bayesian posterior means vs. REML point estimates, confirming that prior influence on the location parameters is minimal given the large sample size.

  • Variance component comparison. Bayesian posterior medians of \(\tau\) and \(\omega\) vs. REML estimates, documenting the expected prior-induced differences and the \(\tau\)-absorption phenomenon in CE models.

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| Chains | 4 | Standard for convergence assessment via $\hat{R}$ and split-$\hat{R}$ |
| Warmup iterations | 2,000 | Sufficient for NUTS adaptation (step size, mass matrix) |
| Sampling iterations | 2,000 | Yields $S = 8{,}000$ total posterior draws |
| Random seed | 20260324 | Exact reproducibility across reruns |
| `adapt_delta` | 0.95 | Conservative target acceptance rate for meta-analysis models |
| `max_treedepth` | 12 | Standard upper bound on leapfrog trajectory length |

The total of $S = 8{,}000$ posterior draws provides adequate effective sample
sizes for all parameters of interest given the relatively low dimensionality of
the models ($K \leq 11$ regression coefficients plus 1--2 variance components).

### Sandwich and Calibration Settings

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| Working correlation ($\rho$) | 0.6 | Recommended by @pustejovsky2022 for social science meta-analyses; sandwich estimator is consistent regardless of $\rho$ choice |
| Small-sample correction | CR2 | Default (Decision D12); bias-reducing leverage adjustment |
| Degrees of freedom | Satterthwaite | Default (Decision D13); accounts for estimation uncertainty in variance components |
| Covariance imputation | `metafor::impute_covariance_matrix()` with `smooth_vi = TRUE` [@viechtbauer2010] | Constructs $V$ matrix assuming equicorrelation within studies at $\rho = 0.6$ |

### Prior Distributions

| Parameter | Prior | Rationale |
|:----------|:------|:----------|
| $\beta_k$ (regression coefficients) | $N(0, 10)$ | Weakly informative for standardized mean differences; prior SD of 10 is effectively flat on the SMD scale |
| $\tau$ (between-study SD) | Half-Cauchy$(0, 0.5)$ | Standard weakly informative prior for heterogeneity in social science; allows substantial heterogeneity while mildly regularizing |
| $\omega$ (within-study SD, CHE only) | Half-Cauchy$(0, 0.5)$ | Same specification as $\tau$; symmetric treatment of variance components |

```r
# CE prior specification
prior_ce <- bm_prior(beta = normal(0, 10), tau = half_cauchy(0, 0.5))

# CHE prior specification
prior_che <- bm_prior(beta = normal(0, 10),
                      tau = half_cauchy(0, 0.5),
                      omega = half_cauchy(0, 0.5))
```

The choice of Half-Cauchy(0, 0.5) for variance component priors follows the
recommendations of @gelman2006 and @polson2012 for hierarchical models. The scale
parameter of 0.5 is appropriate for standardized effect sizes where heterogeneity
SDs rarely exceed 0.5 in most substantive domains, while the heavy tails of the
Cauchy distribution allow the data to dominate when heterogeneity is larger.


## Comparator Estimators {#sec-tsl15-comparators}

The validation compares five estimators that span the frequentist and Bayesian
approaches to meta-analysis with dependent effect sizes. Each estimator pair is
chosen to isolate specific sources of variation in the comparison.

### Estimator Summary

| Estimator | Package | Model | Variance Estimation | df Method | Role |
|:----------|:--------|:------|:-------------------|:----------|:-----|
| CE robumeta | `robumeta::robu` | CE working model | CR2 [@tipton2015] | Satterthwaite | Published baseline |
| CE metafor | `metafor::rma.mv` | CE ($\tau^2$ only) | CR2 via `clubSandwich` | Satterthwaite | Fair CE comparison |
| CHE metafor | `metafor::rma.mv` | CHE ($\tau^2 + \omega^2$) | CR2 via `clubSandwich` | Satterthwaite | Fair CHE comparison |
| bayesRVE CE | `bayesRVE::bm_fit` | CE ($\tau^2$ only) | CCC with CR2 target | Satterthwaite | This package (CE) |
| bayesRVE CHE | `bayesRVE::bm_fit` | CHE ($\tau^2 + \omega^2$) | CCC with CR2 target | Satterthwaite | This package (CHE) |

### Estimator Descriptions

**CE robumeta.** The `robumeta::robu` function implements the correlated-effects
working model of @hedges2010, computing study-level
aggregated effect sizes under an assumed equicorrelation $\rho$, then applying
CR2 small-sample corrections following @tipton2015. This estimator serves as
the published baseline, replicating the exact results reported in @pustejovsky2022. Because robumeta performs study-mean aggregation internally,
its point estimates and standard errors may differ slightly from observation-level
estimators.

**CE metafor.** The `metafor::rma.mv` function [@viechtbauer2010] fits a single-$\tau^2$ random-effects
model at the observation level using REML, with the imputed covariance matrix $V$
incorporating the working correlation $\rho = 0.6$. CR2 standard errors and
Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are obtained via the `clubSandwich` package.
This estimator provides a fair comparison target for bayesRVE CE because both
operate on the same data structure (observation-level, single random effect)
and apply the same CR2 + Satterthwaite inference framework.

**CHE metafor.** The `metafor::rma.mv` function with a two-level random structure
(`~ 1 | studyid / esid`) fits the correlated-hierarchical effects model,
estimating both $\tau^2$ (between-study) and $\omega^2$ (within-study)
via REML. CR2 corrections and Satterthwaite degrees of freedom are again
obtained via `clubSandwich`. This estimator is the fair comparison target
for bayesRVE CHE.

**bayesRVE CE.** The `bayesRVE::bm_fit` function with `model = "ce"` fits a
Bayesian CE model via Stan [@carpenter2017] (NUTS sampler), estimating posterior distributions
for all regression coefficients and $\tau$. The Cholesky Calibration Correction
(CCC) maps posterior draws to have the CR2 sandwich covariance as their
dispersion target. Satterthwaite degrees of freedom define the reference
distribution for inference (Three-Layer Calibration: L1 = posterior mean,
L2 = CR2 sandwich, L3 = Satterthwaite $t$).

**bayesRVE CHE.** The `bayesRVE::bm_fit` function with `model = "che"` extends
the CE model with a within-study variance component $\omega^2$. The CHE CCC
computation uses $\hat{\omega}^2 = \text{mean}(\omega^{(s)2})$ (Decision D20)
to construct the CR2 sandwich with Woodbury-based $W^{1/2}$ weights
(Decision D24). This estimator is the primary new capability introduced in
v0.6.0.

### Comparison Logic

The five-estimator design enables the following structured comparisons:

1. **CE robumeta vs. CE metafor:** Quantifies the effect of study-mean
   aggregation (robumeta) versus observation-level modeling (metafor) within
   the CE framework. Differences in point estimates arise from the distinct
   weighting schemes.

2. **CE metafor vs. bayesRVE CE:** The primary validation comparison for CE
   models. Both use observation-level data with a single random effect.
   Differences reflect the Bayesian-vs-REML estimation and the CCC
   calibration procedure. If CCC is working correctly, the CCC SD should
   approximate the CR2 SE.

3. **CHE metafor vs. bayesRVE CHE:** The primary validation comparison for
   CHE models, introduced in v0.6.0. Both use two random effects. Agreement
   validates that the CHE extension of CCC (omega-aware weights, Woodbury
   $W^{1/2}$) produces calibrated inference.

4. **CE vs. CHE (within estimator):** Reveals the effect of modeling
   within-study heterogeneity. In the CE model, $\tau^2$ absorbs $\omega^2$,
   producing larger between-study variance estimates. The CHE model decomposes
   total heterogeneity, generally yielding similar or slightly different
   standard errors depending on the relative magnitudes of $\tau^2$ and
   $\omega^2$.


## Reproducibility and Decision Rules {#sec-tsl15-decisions}

### Single-Script Reproducibility

The complete analysis---from data preparation through all 14 model fits to
summary tables and figures---is contained in a single R script:

```
dev/log/201_real-data-v060/00_full_analysis.R
```

This script loads the raw dataset, applies all filters and transformations,
fits all frequentist baselines and bayesRVE models, computes all comparison
tables, and saves results to disk. No manual intervention or intermediate files
are required. Given the fixed random seed (`SEED = 20260324`), all MCMC results
are exactly reproducible on the same hardware and software configuration.

### Pre-Specified Decision Rules

All model specifications, MCMC settings, prior distributions, and evaluation
criteria were fixed before running the bayesRVE models. Specifically:

- The two moderator specifications (DV Category, Control Type) replicate
  @pustejovsky2022 exactly.
- The covariate centering scheme follows the original publication.
- Prior distributions and MCMC settings were chosen based on general
  recommendations in the literature, not tuned to the TSL15 data.
- No post-hoc model selection, predictor selection, or prior adjustment
  was performed.

### Gate 3 Criterion

The primary success criterion for the real-data validation is:

$$
R_{\text{SE},k} = \frac{\text{CCC SD}_k}{\text{CR2 SE}_k} \in [0.9, 1.1]
\quad \text{for all DV category coefficients } k = 1, \ldots, 6
$$

This criterion is evaluated separately for CE and CHE models:

- **CE:** CCC SD from bayesRVE CE vs. CR2 SE from metafor CE (via clubSandwich)
- **CHE:** CCC SD from bayesRVE CHE vs. CR2 SE from metafor CHE (via clubSandwich)

Gate 3 is defined exclusively for the six DV category coefficients ($k = 1, \ldots, 6$), which serve as intercepts in the DV-moderator specification and each draw information from a large number of contributing studies. Covariate coefficients (e.g., `percoll_c`, `permale_c`) are reported separately in @sec-mechanics-covariates and are *not* subject to the Gate 3 acceptance band; their CCC/CR2 ratios may fall outside [0.9, 1.1] due to lower effective cluster counts and Satterthwaite df.

### Rationale for the [0.9, 1.1] Band

The 10% tolerance band around unity accommodates two sources of
discrepancy between the Bayesian CCC standard deviations and the frequentist
CR2 standard errors:

1. **Variance-component plug-in discrepancy (primary).** The CCC uses the
   Bayesian posterior mean of variance components ($\tau^2$, $\omega^2$) as
   plug-in estimates for the weight matrix $W$ and hence the bread matrix
   ($X'WX$), while the frequentist CR2 uses REML estimates. Because the
   posterior mean and REML estimate differ due to prior influence, the two
   methods construct different $V_{\text{CR2}}$ targets. By Theorem 2, CCC
   achieves its own $V_{\text{CR2}}$ exactly; the observed ratio $\neq 1$
   reflects two different targets, not a calibration failure. This typically
   contributes 1--5% discrepancy.

2. **MCMC sampling noise (secondary).** Even with $S = 8{,}000$ posterior
   draws, the sample covariance of the calibrated draws has finite Monte
   Carlo error. For well-behaved posteriors, this contributes approximately
   1--2% variation and is reducible by increasing $S$.

A ratio of exactly 1.0 would indicate perfect calibration; the [0.9, 1.1] band
reflects the practical precision achievable given these two sources of variation.
This tolerance was established a priori based on experience from the simulation
studies (sim3, 24 conditions $\times$ 500 replications), where CCC-$t$ coverage achieved 94.5--95.3% nominal
95% coverage with SE ratios in this range.

### Supplementary Diagnostics

Beyond the primary Gate 3 criterion, the following diagnostics are examined:

- **Shrinkage ratio $R_k$.** The ratio of CCC SD to raw posterior SD, quantifying
  how much the calibration correction adjusts each coefficient's uncertainty.
  Values below 1.0 indicate CCC shrinkage (the raw posterior is overestimating
  uncertainty relative to the sandwich target); values above 1.0 indicate CCC
  inflation.

- **Satterthwaite df agreement.** The Satterthwaite degrees of freedom from
  bayesRVE vs. those from clubSandwich, verifying that the independent
  implementation (Decision D11) produces consistent results.

- **Point estimate concordance.** Bayesian posterior means vs. REML point
  estimates, confirming that prior influence on the location parameters is
  minimal given the large sample size.

- **Variance component comparison.** Bayesian posterior medians of $\tau$ and
  $\omega$ vs. REML estimates, documenting the expected prior-induced
  differences and the $\tau$-absorption phenomenon in CE models.
